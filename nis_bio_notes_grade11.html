Global Climate Change
▼

Causes of Global Warming

Combustion of fossil fuels → CO₂↑ (greenhouse gas)
Deforestation → less CO₂ absorbed
Agriculture → CH₄ (methane from rice fields, livestock) and N₂O
Industrial processes → CFC, HFC (ozone-depleting)

Consequences

Rising sea levels (melting ice caps) → coastal flooding
More extreme weather events (hurricanes, droughts, floods)
Shifts in species distribution; habitat loss; extinction risk
Ocean acidification (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃) → harm to coral, molluscs
Altered agricultural zones; food security risks
Kazakhstan-specific issues: Aral Sea shrinkage, desertification, industrial pollution from mining/smelting, steppe degradation. Solutions: reforestation, renewable energy, water management.

Surface Area : Volume Ratio
▼
SA:V ratio = Surface area ÷ Volume
As cell size increases → SA:V ratio decreases
Smaller cells have larger SA:V → more efficient exchange of gases and nutrients
Very large cells need internal transport systems (e.g. ER, cytoplasmic streaming)
Adaptations for exchange: microvilli (intestine), alveoli, root hair cells → all increase SA
Cube of side 1 cm: SA = 6 cm², V = 1 cm³, SA:V = 6. Cube of side 2 cm: SA = 24, V = 8, SA:V = 3. Doubling size → ratio halves.

Water — Properties & Biological Significance
▼

Why Water is Polar

Oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen → electrons are pulled towards O → O carries partial negative charge (δ−); H carries partial positive charge (δ+). This polarity allows water molecules to form hydrogen bonds with each other and with other polar molecules.

Biologically Important Properties

PropertyExplanationBiological significance
High specific heat capacityMany H-bonds must be broken to raise temperatureStable aquatic habitat; body temperature regulation
High latent heat of vaporisationMuch energy needed to evaporate waterSweating/transpiration cools organisms efficiently
CohesionH-bonds between water moleculesWater columns in xylem; surface tension
AdhesionWater sticks to polar surfacesCapillary action in plants
SolventPolar water dissolves ionic/polar substancesMedium for biochemical reactions; transport
TransparentLight passes throughPhotosynthesis in aquatic plants
Ice less dense than waterH-bonds in ice form regular latticeIce floats → insulates water below; aquatic life survives
Proteins — Structure & Function
▼

4 Levels of Protein Structure

LevelDescriptionBonds
Primary (1°)Sequence of amino acids in polypeptide chainPeptide bonds (covalent)
Secondary (2°)α-helix or β-pleated sheet; local foldingHydrogen bonds between backbone C=O and N-H
Tertiary (3°)3D overall shape of polypeptide; folding of secondary structuresH-bonds, disulfide bridges (S–S), ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions
Quaternary (4°)Multiple polypeptide chains assembledSame as tertiary + between subunits

Haemoglobin

HAEMOGLOBINQuaternary structure: 4 polypeptide chains (2α + 2β), each with a haem group containing Fe²⁺. Each haem binds one O₂. Total: 4 O₂ per haemoglobin molecule. Cooperative binding → sigmoidal dissociation curve.

Collagen

COLLAGENQuaternary structure: 3 polypeptide chains wound into a triple helix; rich in glycine, proline, hydroxyproline. Very strong (tensile strength). Found in skin, tendons, bones, blood vessels. Insoluble → structural role.
Haemoglobin = globular (soluble, spherical, functional role). Collagen = fibrous (insoluble, structural role). Both are quaternary. Key difference: haemoglobin has haem prosthetic group; collagen is purely protein.

Protein Denaturation

Denaturation — disruption of non-covalent bonds (H-bonds, ionic bonds) → loss of 3D shape → loss of function. Peptide bonds NOT broken.
Caused by: high temperature (breaks H-bonds), extreme pH (disrupts ionic interactions)
Renaturation — possible if conditions are restored gently; primary structure intact → can refold (e.g. ribonuclease A)
Irreversible denaturation: e.g. cooking egg white (albumin) — protein aggregates
Lipids — Structure & Functions
▼

Phospholipid Structure

Head: glycerol + phosphate group — hydrophilic (water-loving)
Tails: 2 fatty acid chains — hydrophobic (water-repelling)
Amphipathic → spontaneously forms bilayer in water
Saturated fatty acids: no double bonds; straight chains; solid at room temp; more stable
Unsaturated fatty acids: one or more C=C double bonds; kinked chains; liquid at room temp (oils)

Functions of Lipids

Energy storage (triglycerides): 2× energy per gram compared to carbohydrates
Membrane structure (phospholipids, cholesterol)
Insulation: thermal (adipose tissue) and electrical (myelin sheath)
Hormones: steroid hormones (sex hormones, cortisol) are lipid-derived
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) transport and absorption

Nucleic Acids & Protein Biosynthesis

DNA Structure
▼

Nucleotide Structure

Deoxyribose sugar (5-carbon, pentose)
Phosphate group (attached to 5' carbon)
Nitrogenous base: Purines (A, G — double ring) or Pyrimidines (T, C — single ring)

DNA Double Helix (Watson & Crick, 1953)

Two antiparallel polynucleotide strands: one runs 5'→3', the other 3'→5'
Strands held by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases: A=T (2 H-bonds); G≡C (3 H-bonds)
Sugar-phosphate backbone on outside; bases point inward
Chargaff's rules: %A = %T; %G = %C in any DNA sample

DNA Replication
▼
SEMI-CONSERVATIVE REPLICATIONEach daughter DNA molecule contains one original (parental) strand and one newly synthesised strand. Proved by Meselson & Stahl (1958) using ¹⁵N/¹⁴N isotope labelling.

Steps of Replication

01
Helicase unwinds and separates (unzips) double helix at the replication fork; breaks H-bonds between base pairs
02
Primase adds short RNA primer (provides free 3'-OH for DNA polymerase)
03
DNA polymerase III adds complementary deoxyribonucleotides in 5'→3' direction; uses each strand as template
04
Leading strand: synthesised continuously (same direction as replication fork); Lagging strand: synthesised discontinuously as Okazaki fragments
05
DNA polymerase I removes RNA primers; fills gaps
06
DNA ligase joins Okazaki fragments (seals nicks in sugar-phosphate backbone)
DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end of an existing strand (needs a primer). It cannot synthesise DNA de novo.
Genetic Code Properties
▼
Triplet — 3 nucleotides (codon) codes for 1 amino acid
Degenerate — most amino acids coded by more than one codon (64 codons; only 20 amino acids). Protects against mutations.
Non-overlapping — each base read only once; belongs to only one codon
Universal — same code in virtually all organisms (evidence of common ancestry)
Unambiguous — each codon codes for only one specific amino acid
Start codon: AUG (codes for methionine); Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (no amino acid)

Oxygen Dissociation Curves & Bohr Effect
▼

Oxygen Dissociation Curve

S-shaped (sigmoidal) because of cooperative binding: binding of first O₂ makes subsequent binding easier (conformational change)
High pO₂ (e.g. lungs, 13 kPa) → haemoglobin almost fully saturated (~98%)
Low pO₂ (e.g. respiring tissues, 2–5 kPa) → haemoglobin releases O₂ readily
Foetal haemoglobin (HbF): curve shifted LEFT → higher affinity for O₂ → takes O₂ from maternal HbA across placenta
Myoglobin: hyperbolic curve; even higher affinity; stores O₂ in muscle; releases only at very low pO₂
BOHR EFFECTAn increase in CO₂ concentration (from respiring tissues) lowers blood pH (CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻). H⁺ ions bind to haemoglobin → change in shape → lower affinity for O₂ → O₂ released more readily to tissues that need it. The dissociation curve shifts RIGHT.
Bohr shift is adaptive: most active tissues produce most CO₂ → most O₂ released where it is most needed. This is the basis of the "Explain the importance of the Bohr effect for respiring muscle cells" question type.

Respiration & Excretion

ATP · Glycolysis · Krebs · ETC · Oxidative phosphorylation · Nephron · ADH · Dialysis

ATP — Structure & Function
▼
ADENOSINE TRIPHOSPHATE (ATP)Universal energy currency of the cell. Consists of: adenine (nitrogenous base) + ribose (5-carbon sugar) + 3 phosphate groups. Hydrolysis of the terminal phosphate bond (by ATPase) releases ~30.5 kJ/mol energy.
ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pi + energy (30.5 kJ/mol)

Why ATP is the Perfect Energy Currency

Releases manageable amounts of energy (not too much at once)
Easily regenerated from ADP + Pi by cellular respiration
Soluble — easily transported within cell
Cannot leave the cell → energy stays where needed
Stages of Aerobic Respiration
▼

Stage 1 — Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)

01
Phosphorylation: Glucose (6C) + 2ATP → glucose-6-phosphate → fructose-1,6-bisphosphate
02
Splitting: Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (6C) → 2× triose phosphate (3C)
03
Oxidation/substrate phosphorylation: Triose phosphate oxidised → 2× pyruvate; produces 4 ATP and 2 NADH
Glucose → 2 Pyruvate + 2 ATP (net) + 2 NADH

Stage 2 — Link Reaction (Mitochondrial Matrix)

Pyruvate (3C) + CoA → Acetyl-CoA (2C) + CO₂ + NADH
Decarboxylation (CO₂ removed) + dehydrogenation (NADH formed)
No ATP produced at this stage

Stage 3 — Krebs Cycle (Matrix)

Acetyl-CoA (2C) + oxaloacetate (4C) → citrate (6C)
2 turns per glucose; per turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 ATP, 2 CO₂ released
Oxaloacetate regenerated at end of each turn (cycle)
Substrate-level phosphorylation produces 1 ATP per turn

Stage 4 — Oxidative Phosphorylation (Inner Mitochondrial Membrane)

01
NADH and FADH₂ donate electrons to electron transport chain (Complex I, II, III, IV)
02
Energy from electrons pumps H⁺ ions from matrix → intermembrane space
03
H⁺ gradient (proton motive force) drives H⁺ back through ATP synthase → ATP synthesis (chemiosmosis)
04
O₂ = final electron acceptor: O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂O
Total ATP yield (aerobic): ~38 ATP per glucose molecule

FeatureIn animals/fungi (lactate)In plants/yeast (ethanol)

Nephron & Kidney Function
▼

Urine Formation — 3 Processes

ProcessLocationWhat happens
UltrafiltrationGlomerulus / Bowman's capsuleBlood pressure forces small molecules (water, glucose, urea, ions, amino acids) out of blood into Bowman's capsule. Large proteins and red blood cells stay.
Selective reabsorptionProximal convoluted tubule (mainly)All glucose, most water, amino acids, some ions actively transported back into blood. Microvilli (brush border) increase SA. Mitochondria provide ATP.
ConcentrationLoop of Henle, DCT, collecting ductLoop of Henle creates osmotic gradient in medulla. ADH controls water reabsorption in collecting duct.
ADH (ANTIDIURETIC HORMONE)Released from posterior pituitary when blood osmolarity increases (detected by hypothalamus osmoreceptors). ADH increases permeability of collecting duct to water (inserts aquaporin channels) → more water reabsorbed → concentrated urine produced. Low ADH → dilute urine.

Haemodialysis

Blood removed from patient, passed through semi-permeable dialysis membrane
Dialysate fluid has same composition as normal blood (no urea, correct glucose/ion levels)
Urea and excess ions diffuse out of blood into dialysate (down concentration gradient)
Glucose and useful substances do NOT diffuse out (same concentration both sides)
Heparin added to prevent blood clotting in the machine
Counter-current flow (blood and dialysate flow in opposite directions) → maintains maximum gradient
Dialysis advantage: immediately available, no rejection. Disadvantage: time-consuming (3×/week, 4 hrs each), diet restrictions, not permanent solution. Transplant: more natural, once successful = normal life. Risk: rejection, need immunosuppressants.

Action Potential in Myelinated Neurons
▼

Resting Potential (−70 mV)

Na⁺-K⁺ pump maintains: 3 Na⁺ out per 2 K⁺ in per ATP → inside negative
Membrane more permeable to K⁺ (leak channels) → K⁺ diffuses out → adds to negativity inside

Action Potential Stages

01
Threshold reached (~−55 mV) → voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open
02
Depolarisation: Na⁺ rushes in (electrochemical gradient) → inside becomes positive (+40 mV)
03
Repolarisation: Na⁺ channels close; voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ rushes out → inside becomes negative again
04
Hyperpolarisation: K⁺ channels slow to close → briefly more negative than resting
05
Refractory period: Na⁺ channels inactivated; another action potential cannot be triggered → ensures one-way propagation; limits frequency of impulses

Myelin Sheath

Produced by Schwann cells (PNS) or oligodendrocytes (CNS); wraps around axon
Saltatory conduction: action potential jumps between Nodes of Ranvier (unmyelinated gaps) → much faster than in unmyelinated fibres
Faster conduction; less energy used (Na⁺-K⁺ pump only needed at nodes)
MS (multiple sclerosis): autoimmune destruction of myelin → slowed/blocked impulse transmission
Cholinergic Synapse
▼

Synaptic Transmission Steps

01
Action potential reaches presynaptic terminal
02
Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ ions enter presynaptic terminal
03
Ca²⁺ causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with presynaptic membrane → exocytosis of acetylcholine (ACh)
04
ACh diffuses across synaptic cleft (20 nm)
05
ACh binds to nicotinic receptors on postsynaptic membrane → Na⁺ channels open → depolarisation → new action potential (if threshold reached)
06
Acetylcholinesterase breaks ACh → acetate + choline (prevents continuous stimulation)
07
Choline taken back up by presynaptic terminal → resynthesised into ACh

Role in Coordination

Synapses ensure unidirectional signal transmission (receptors only on postsynaptic side)
Summation: temporal (repeated impulses from one neurone) or spatial (impulses from multiple neurones) → allows threshold to be reached or not
Synapses allow integration of signals: excitatory and inhibitory inputs summed
Pacinian corpuscle: mechanoreceptor; onion-like layers of connective tissue around dendrite ending; deformation of layers opens Na⁺ channels → generator potential → action potential if threshold reached

Cell Cycle & Division

Mitosis · Meiosis · Gametogenesis · Cancer

Mitosis
▼

Cell Cycle Phases

Interphase — G1 (cell growth, protein synthesis) → S phase (DNA replication) → G2 (preparation for division, organelle replication)
M phase — mitosis + cytokinesis

Stages of Mitosis (PMAT)

StageKey Events
ProphaseChromosomes condense (visible); spindle forms from centrioles; nuclear envelope breaks down; nucleolus disappears
MetaphaseChromosomes align at metaphase plate (equator); spindle fibres attach to centromeres
AnaphaseSister chromatids pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibres shortening; centromeres split
TelophaseChromosomes decondense; nuclear envelope reforms; nucleolus reappears; cytokinesis begins
Mitosis produces 2 genetically identical daughter cells. Chromosome number maintained (2n → 2n). Used for: growth, repair, asexual reproduction. NOT meiosis.
Meiosis & Genetic Recombination
▼

Meiosis vs Mitosis

FeatureMitosisMeiosis
Number of divisions12 (Meiosis I + Meiosis II)
Daughter cells24
Chromosome numberSame as parent (2n)Half of parent (n)
Genetic variationNone (genetically identical)Yes (crossing over + independent assortment)

Sources of Genetic Variation in Meiosis

Crossing over (recombination) — during Prophase I: homologous chromosomes (bivalents) exchange segments at chiasmata → new combinations of alleles
Independent assortment — during Metaphase I: homologous pairs align randomly → 2²³ possible combinations in humans
Random fertilisation — any egg can combine with any sperm

Cancer

Uncontrolled cell division due to mutations in proto-oncogenes (become oncogenes) or tumour suppressor genes (e.g. p53)
Causes: mutagens (ionising radiation, UV, carcinogens, viral infections), hereditary mutations
Tumour: benign (does not spread) vs malignant (metastasis — spreads to other tissues)
Cells lose contact inhibition; divide without limit; may avoid apoptosis
Gametogenesis in Plants & Animals
▼

Animal Gametogenesis

Spermatogenesis: spermatogonia → (mitosis) → primary spermatocyte → (meiosis I) → secondary spermatocytes → (meiosis II) → spermatids → (spermiogenesis) → spermatozoa. 4 sperm per meiosis.
Oogenesis: oogonium → primary oocyte (arrested in prophase I until puberty) → (meiosis I at ovulation) → secondary oocyte + first polar body → (meiosis II at fertilisation) → egg + second polar body. 1 egg per meiosis.

Plant Gametogenesis

Sporogenesis (in anthers): diploid microsporocytes → (meiosis) → 4 haploid microspores → (mitosis) → pollen grains (male gametophyte)
Female gametophyte: megaspore mother cell → (meiosis) → 4 megaspores; 3 degenerate; 1 undergoes 3 rounds of mitosis → embryo sac with 7 cells (including egg cell + 2 polar nuclei)
Double fertilisation: one sperm + egg → zygote (embryo); other sperm + 2 polar nuclei → endosperm (3n)

Growth, Ageing & Muscle Movement

Ageing theories · Sliding filament theory · Fast vs slow muscle fibres

Ageing
▼
Ageing is a universal biological law — all multicellular organisms age and die
Telomeric theory: telomeres shorten with each mitosis; eventually reach critical length → cell cannot divide → senescence or apoptosis
Free radical theory: reactive oxygen species (ROS) damage DNA, proteins, and lipids; accumulates over time
Accumulation theory: waste products (e.g. lipofuscin) accumulate in cells → dysfunction
Error catastrophe theory: errors in protein synthesis accumulate → dysfunctional proteins
Genetic theory: genes that promote survival early in life may cause decline later (antagonistic pleiotropy)
Muscle Contraction — Sliding Filament Theory
▼

Sarcomere Structure

Thick filaments: myosin (heads can bind actin and hydrolyse ATP)
Thin filaments: actin + tropomyosin + troponin (regulatory proteins)
A band: region of thick filaments; does not shorten during contraction
I band: actin only; shortens during contraction
H zone: myosin only; disappears during full contraction
Z line: anchors actin; boundaries of sarcomere; Z lines move closer together

Mechanism of Contraction

01
Action potential travels down T-tubules → stimulates sarcoplasmic reticulum → Ca²⁺ released into sarcomere
02
Ca²⁺ binds troponin → conformational change → tropomyosin moves → reveals myosin-binding sites on actin
03
Myosin head (with ADP + Pi) binds actin → forms cross-bridge
04
Power stroke: Pi released → myosin head pivots → actin filament slides toward centre; ADP released
05
New ATP binds myosin head → cross-bridge detaches from actin
06
ATP hydrolysed → myosin head returns to original position → cycle repeats while Ca²⁺ present

MitochondriaManyFew
RespirationMainly aerobicMainly anaerobic glycolysis
Contraction speedSlowFast
Fatigue resistanceHigh (long endurance)Low (fatigues quickly)

Monohybrid Cross (Aa × Aa)

Aa × Aa → 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa (genotypic 1:2:1) → 3 dominant : 1 recessive (phenotypic 3:1)
Cytological basis: alleles are on homologous chromosomes; they separate during meiosis I (Law of Segregation); gametes are haploid

X-Linked Inheritance

Gene on X chromosome; males (XY) only have one copy (hemizygous) → always express X-linked alleles
Carrier females (X^H X^h): normal phenotype, but can pass allele to sons
Examples: haemophilia, colour blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy
X-linked recessive: affects more males than females; never passed father → son (father gives Y to sons)
Chromosome Theory & Linkage
▼
CHROMOSOME THEORY OF HEREDITYGenes are carried on chromosomes. Genes on the same chromosome are linked and tend to be inherited together (do not follow independent assortment independently).

Crossing Over

During Prophase I, homologous chromosomes form bivalents → exchange segments at chiasmata
Creates recombinant chromosomes → new allele combinations not present in parents
The further apart two genes are on the same chromosome, the more likely a crossover will occur between them → higher recombination frequency
Recombination frequency used to map gene distances: 1% recombination = 1 centimorgan (cM)

Natural Selection & Allele Frequency
▼

Mechanisms of Evolution

Natural selection: organisms with advantageous alleles → higher survival and reproduction → alleles increase in frequency. Requires: variation, inheritance, differential reproductive success.
Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequency; most significant in small populations; can fix neutral or even harmful alleles
Founder effect: small group establishes new population → limited gene pool → certain alleles become very common or rare
Bottleneck effect: population drastically reduced → low genetic diversity in survivors
Gene flow: exchange of alleles between populations via migration → tends to homogenise gene pools
Population waves: periodic fluctuations in population size → affects genetic diversity

Types of Natural Selection

TypeEffect on distributionExample
StabilisingExtremes selected against; intermediate favoured; reduces variationBirth weight in humans
DirectionalOne extreme selected for; distribution shiftsAntibiotic resistance, industrial melanism
DisruptiveBoth extremes favoured; middle selected against; can lead to speciationBeak size in African seedcrackers

Speciation

Allopatric speciation: geographical isolation (e.g. mountain range, sea) → populations evolve separately → reproductive isolation → new species
Sympatric speciation: same geographical area; reproductive isolation via polyploidy (common in plants) or behavioural/ecological isolation
Species: group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring

Closed vessel for controlled growth of microorganisms at large scale

PCR — Polymerase Chain Reaction
▼
PCRIn vitro technique to amplify a specific DNA sequence exponentially. Starting from tiny amounts of DNA (even from a single cell), millions of copies can be made in 2–3 hours.

PCR Cycle (Thermocycling)

01
Denaturation (94–96°C): H-bonds between DNA strands broken → two single strands
02
Annealing (50–65°C): short synthetic primers bind to complementary sequences on each strand (flank the target region)
03
Extension/Elongation (72°C): Taq polymerase (thermostable, from Thermus aquaticus) extends from primers; adds complementary nucleotides 5'→3'
04
Cycle repeats ~30–40 times → 2ⁿ copies after n cycles (exponential amplification)

Applications of PCR

Forensics: DNA profiling from trace evidence (blood, hair, saliva)
Medical diagnosis: detect pathogens (HIV, COVID-19); identify genetic mutations
Paternity testing
Palaeontology: amplify DNA from fossils/archaeological remains
Gene cloning: produce large amounts of target gene for genetic engineering
Genetic Engineering & GMOs
▼

Steps of Genetic Engineering

01
Identify target gene: using gene libraries, mRNA → cDNA (via reverse transcriptase)
02
Isolate gene: restriction endonucleases cut at specific recognition sites → sticky ends
03
Insert into vector: same restriction enzyme cuts plasmid; DNA ligase joins gene into plasmid → recombinant plasmid
04
Transform host cell: bacteria take up plasmid (heat shock, electroporation)
05
Select transformants: using antibiotic resistance markers or fluorescent markers
06
Express gene: grow in fermentor; extract and purify protein product

GMO Examples & Uses

Human insulin produced by E. coli with human INS gene
Bt crops: Bacillus thuringiensis toxin gene in maize → insect resistance
Golden rice: β-carotene genes inserted → vitamin A production
HGH (human growth hormone), clotting factors, vaccines
Ethical issues of GMOs: unpredictable effects on ecosystems; gene transfer to wild plants (gene flow); loss of biodiversity; corporate control of food supply; long-term health effects unknown; "playing God" arguments. Counter: reduce pesticide use, drought resistance, nutritional enhancement, reduced food insecurity.
