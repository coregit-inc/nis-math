01Physical Quantities & Measurement

Foundation of physics: how we describe the physical world quantitatively, and how reliable our measurements are.

The SI (Système International) system is built on seven base units, from which all others are derived:

Derived units are combinations of base units: e.g., N = kg·m·s⁻², J = kg·m²·s⁻², W = kg·m²·s⁻³, V = kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻¹, C = A·s.

Vectors and Scalars

Vector: quantity with both magnitude and direction (displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, field strength).
Scalar: quantity with magnitude only (distance, speed, mass, energy, time).

Vector addition methods:

  • Triangle method: place vectors head-to-tail; resultant runs from start to end
  • Parallelogram method: draw vectors from common origin; resultant = diagonal
  • Resolution: any vector $F$ at angle α can be split into components $F\cos\alpha$ (horizontal) and $F\sin\alpha$ (vertical)

Checking Equations (Homogeneity)

An equation is homogeneous ("balanced") when every term has the same base units. If not, the equation is wrong. Example: in $v = u + at$, every term has units m·s⁻¹.

Errors in Measurement

Systematic error: constant offset in all readings (e.g., zero error of an instrument). Reduces accuracy. Cannot be eliminated by averaging. Fix: correct reading = reading obtained − zero error.
Random error: readings scattered about the true value (parallax, observer reaction). Reduces precision. Fix: repeat experiment and take average.

Accuracy vs Precision

  • Accuracy: how close measurements are to the true value
  • Precision: how close repeated measurements are to each other (small random error)

Calculating Uncertainties

For repeated measurements $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$:

$$x_{mean} = \frac{x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n}{n}, \quad \Delta x = \frac{x_{max}-x_{min}}{2}$$

Result: $x = x_{mean} \pm \Delta x$. Percentage uncertainty: $\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta x}{x_{mean}} \times 100\%$.

Combining uncertainties

  • Addition/subtraction: add absolute uncertainties
  • Multiplication/division: add percentage (relative) uncertainties
  • Powers ($x^n$): multiply the percentage uncertainty by $n$
  • Logarithms: if $y = \ln x$, then $\Delta y = \ln(x_{max}) - \ln(x_{mean})$

Significant Figures

  • Leftmost non-zero digit is most significant
  • Without a decimal: rightmost non-zero is least significant
  • With a decimal: rightmost digit is least significant (even if zero)
  • Add/subtract: answer matches fewest decimal places
  • Multiply/divide: answer matches fewest significant figures

02Kinematics

Description of motion without considering its causes.

Key Quantities

Displacement: distance in a specified direction from start to finish (vector).
Distance: total path length traveled (scalar).
Velocity: displacement per unit time = $\Delta s / \Delta t$ (vector).
Speed: distance per unit time (scalar).
Acceleration: change in velocity per unit time = $(v - u)/t$ (vector).

Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion (SUVAT)

$$v = u + at \qquad s = \frac{(u+v)t}{2}$$ $$s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \qquad v^2 = u^2 + 2as$$

Where $u$ = initial velocity, $v$ = final velocity, $a$ = acceleration, $t$ = time, $s$ = displacement.

Curve shape tells you the motion type: straight line = uniform; concave up = increasing rate; concave down = decreasing rate.

Free Fall

Motion under gravity alone (air resistance negligible), $g = 9.81$ m/s².

$$v = u + gt, \quad h = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2, \quad v^2 = u^2 + 2gh$$

From rest ($u = 0$): $v = gt$, $h = gt^2/2$, $v = \sqrt{2gh}$, $t = \sqrt{2h/g}$.

Projectile Motion

Motion with constant horizontal velocity AND constant vertical acceleration $g$ downward (assuming negligible air resistance).

Horizontal (no force, $a_x = 0$):

$$v_{0x} = v_0\cos\alpha, \quad v_x = v_0\cos\alpha = \text{const}, \quad x = v_0\cos\alpha \cdot t$$

Vertical (gravity, $a_y = -g$):

$$v_{0y} = v_0\sin\alpha, \quad v_y = v_0\sin\alpha - gt, \quad y = v_0\sin\alpha \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$$
Key insights:
  • Path is a parabola
  • Horizontal and vertical motions are independent but share the same time
  • At maximum height: $v_y = 0$, but $v_x \neq 0$
  • Vertical velocity is greatest at launch and just before impact

03Forces & Momentum

Why objects accelerate, and how their interactions are governed by conservation laws.

Linear Momentum

Momentum $p = mv$. Vector quantity, units kg·m/s. Direction same as velocity.

Newton's Second Law (general form)

The net force on an object equals the rate of change of its linear momentum, in the same direction.
$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{m\Delta v}{\Delta t} = ma$$

The "ma" form assumes constant mass. For variable-mass systems (e.g., rockets), use the momentum form.

Impulse

Impulse = force × time = change in momentum: $F \cdot \Delta t = \Delta p$. Units: N·s = kg·m/s.

Geometrically: area under a force-time graph equals impulse.

Conservation of Momentum

In a closed system (no external forces), the total momentum in any direction is constant.
$$m_1u_1 + m_2u_2 = m_1v_1 + m_2v_2$$

Momentum is conserved in both elastic and inelastic collisions.

Types of Collisions

Elastic collision: total kinetic energy conserved. Equivalent test: relative speed of approach = relative speed of separation: $u_1 - u_2 = v_2 - v_1$.
Inelastic collision: some KE converted to heat, sound, deformation. Momentum still conserved. Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together after collision.
Why does momentum conservation work? By Newton's third law, internal forces in a closed system come in equal and opposite pairs. Their impulses cancel, so total momentum cannot change unless an external force acts.

04Circular Motion & Gravitation

Motion along curved paths and the universal force that keeps planets in orbit.

Uniform Circular Motion

Object moving at constant speed along a circle. Velocity direction changes constantly → acceleration exists.

$$T = \frac{1}{f}, \quad \omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi f, \quad v = \omega r$$

Centripetal Acceleration & Force

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r, \quad F_c = ma_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$
  • Velocity is always tangential to the circle
  • Acceleration points toward the center of the circle
  • $\vec{v} \perp \vec{a}$ at every point
"Centripetal force" is not a new force — it is the net resultant force (gravity, tension, normal, friction, etc.) that produces circular motion.

Newton's Law of Gravitation

Two point masses attract each other with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of their separation.
$$F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}, \quad G = 6.67\times 10^{-11} \text{ N·m}^2/\text{kg}^2$$

Gravitational Field Strength

g at a point = gravitational force per unit mass on a small test mass at that point.
$$g = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$

Field lines point toward the source mass; their density indicates field strength.

Gravitational Potential Energy & Potential

Gravitational potential $\varphi$ at a point = work done per unit mass in bringing a small mass from infinity to that point.
$$E_p = -\frac{GMm}{r}, \quad \varphi = -\frac{GM}{r}$$
Why negative? By convention, potential at infinity is zero. Work done by gravity in bringing mass from infinity is positive, so the potential closer to the mass is below zero.

Circular Orbits

Gravity provides the centripetal force:

Derivation

$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow v^2 = \frac{GM}{r} \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$

Also $v = 2\pi r/T$, so $\frac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2} = \frac{GM}{r} \Rightarrow T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}$ (Kepler's third law).

Escape Velocity

Minimum velocity needed to escape a gravitational field to infinity.

Derivation

At escape, total energy = 0: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 - \frac{GMm}{r} = 0 \Rightarrow v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$

For Earth: $v_{esc} \approx 11.2$ km/s.

05Fluids

Substances that can flow — liquids and gases. Same physics, different applications.

Density and Pressure

$$\rho = \frac{m}{V}, \quad p = \frac{F}{A}$$

Relative density = ρ(substance) / ρ(water at 4°C). Dimensionless.

Pressure in a Static Fluid

$$p = \rho g h$$
  • Pressure increases with depth
  • All points at the same depth in the same fluid are at the same pressure
  • Force from pressure is always perpendicular to the surface, regardless of orientation

Pascal's Principle

Any pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every part of the fluid and to the walls of the container.

Application — hydraulics: $\frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2}$. Small force on small piston produces large force on large piston.

Archimedes' Principle

An object immersed in a fluid experiences an upward force (upthrust) equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces.
$$F_{up} = \rho_{fluid} \cdot V_{displaced} \cdot g$$

Manometer & Balancing Columns

For a U-tube with two immiscible liquids, points at the same level in the lower fluid are at the same pressure: $\rho_1 h_1 = \rho_2 h_2$.

Fluid Dynamics — Key Concepts

Incompressible fluid: density doesn't change with pressure. Liquids are essentially incompressible.
Viscosity ($\eta$): a fluid's resistance to flow due to internal friction. Decreases with temperature for liquids.
Ideal fluid: incompressible AND non-viscous (η = 0).

Streamlines & Types of Flow

  • Streamline: a curve tangent to fluid velocity at every point. Streamlines never cross.
  • Steady flow: velocity at every point is constant in time
  • Laminar flow: fluid moves in parallel layers without mixing
  • Turbulent flow: chaotic, eddies, velocity fluctuates
$$R_e = \frac{\rho v D}{\eta}$$

Reynolds number $R_e < 2000$ → laminar; $R_e > 3000\text{–}5000$ → turbulent.

Equation of Continuity

For incompressible fluid in steady flow, mass flow rate is constant:

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

Where pipe narrows, fluid speeds up.

Bernoulli's Equation

For incompressible, non-viscous fluid in steady flow: pressure + KE per unit volume + PE per unit volume = constant along a streamline. (This is energy conservation.)
$$p + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g h = \text{const}$$

Bernoulli's principle: where speed is high, pressure is low (and vice versa). Explains lift on aircraft wings, curveballs, and venturi tubes.

Validity: incompressible, non-viscous, steady flow. For horizontal flow, height term drops out.

Torricelli's Theorem

Speed of fluid leaving a hole at depth $h$ below the surface (open tank):

$$v = \sqrt{2g\Delta h}$$

Same as if fluid had fallen freely through that height.

Pitot-Static Tube

Measures fluid velocity. Combines a Pitot tube (facing flow, stagnation point) with a static tube (perpendicular). The pressure difference relates to velocity:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2(p_2 - p_1)}{\rho}}$$

Used in aircraft airspeed indicators.

06Deforming Solids

How solids respond to applied forces, and the energy stored in their deformation.

Compressive vs Tensile Forces

  • Tensile: pull on opposite ends → object stretches
  • Compressive: push on opposite ends → object compresses

Elastic vs Plastic Behavior

Elastic: object returns to original shape when force removed.
Plastic: permanent deformation remains after force is removed.

Hooke's Law

For a spring within its limit of proportionality, the extension is directly proportional to the applied force.
$$F = -k\Delta l$$

$k$ = spring constant (N/m). The minus sign: restoring force opposes the displacement.

On a force-extension graph: P = limit of proportionality (Hooke's law fails beyond P), E = elastic limit (plastic deformation begins beyond E).

Stress, Strain, Young Modulus

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \quad \text{(stress, Pa)}$$ $$\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta l}{l_0} \quad \text{(strain, dimensionless)}$$ $$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} \quad \text{(Young modulus, Pa)}$$

Young modulus is a material property — independent of object shape/size.

Determining Young Modulus (Experiment)

  1. Measure original length $l_0$ with metre ruler
  2. Measure wire diameter with micrometer (multiple positions, average); $A = \pi d^2/4$
  3. Hang masses, measure extension with reference mark and ruler
  4. Plot $F$ vs $\Delta l$; gradient = $k = EA/l_0$
  5. Calculate $E = \text{gradient} \times l_0 / A$

Brittle vs Ductile Materials

Brittle (glass, cast iron): elastic up to breaking point, no plastic region. Snap suddenly.
Ductile (copper, gold): show plastic flow before breaking. Can be drawn into wires, hammered into shapes.

Elastic Potential Energy

$$E_{elastic} = \tfrac{1}{2}k(\Delta l)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}F\Delta l$$

This is the area under the F-x graph (for a Hookean spring, a triangle).

07Oscillations (SHM)

Periodic motion that repeats: pendulums, springs, atoms in solids, and the basis of all wave physics.

Definitions

Free oscillation: oscillation without external driving force or resistive forces. Frequency = natural frequency.
Forced oscillation: oscillation under a periodic external driving force.
Period (T): time for one complete oscillation. Frequency (f): oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$.
Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from equilibrium.

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

A body executes SHM if its acceleration is directly proportional to its displacement from equilibrium and always directed toward equilibrium.
$$a = -\omega^2 x$$

The minus sign means restoring direction. SHM has two key properties:

  • $x$, $v$, $a$ vary sinusoidally with time
  • Period (frequency) is independent of amplitude

Equations of Motion

$$x = A\sin\omega t$$ $$v = A\omega\cos\omega t \quad (v_{max} = A\omega)$$ $$a = -A\omega^2\sin\omega t \quad (a_{max} = A\omega^2)$$

Velocity = gradient of x-t graph; acceleration = gradient of v-t graph.

Period of Specific SHM Systems

Mass-spring: $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$ Independent of amplitude AND of gravity.
Simple pendulum: $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}$$ Independent of mass, valid for small angles only.

Why mass-spring SHM

$F = -kx$ and $F = ma$ → $a = -\frac{k}{m}x$. Compare with $a = -\omega^2 x$ → $\omega^2 = k/m$ → $T = 2\pi/\omega = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$.

Energy in SHM

For free SHM, total energy is constant; KE and PE alternate.

  • KE maximum at $x = 0$ (equilibrium): $KE_{max} = \tfrac{1}{2}mA^2\omega^2$
  • PE maximum at $x = \pm A$ (extremes)
  • Total: $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$ (constant)

Damping

When resistive forces present, mechanical energy converts to thermal energy, amplitude decreases (exponentially for light damping).

  • Light damping: amplitude decays exponentially over many oscillations
  • Critical damping: system returns to equilibrium in shortest time without oscillating (e.g., car suspension)
  • Heavy (over) damping: very slow return to equilibrium, no oscillation

Resonance

Large amplitude increase when driving frequency equals (or is very close to) the natural frequency of the system.

Useful resonance: MRI, microwave ovens (water natural freq), quartz watches, musical instruments.

Avoid resonance: bridge oscillations (Tacoma Narrows), building sway in earthquakes/wind, vibration in machinery. Solutions: tuned mass dampers, stiffening, streamlining.

08Molecular Physics

Macroscopic gas behavior emerging from microscopic molecular motion.

Key Definitions

Mole: amount of substance containing the same number of atoms as 12 g of carbon-12.
Avogadro constant: $N_A = 6.02 \times 10^{23}$ mol⁻¹.
Standard temperature and pressure (STP): $T = 273$ K, $p = 1.013 \times 10^5$ Pa. 1 mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L at STP.

Useful Relationships

$$N = nN_A, \quad n = \frac{N}{N_A} = \frac{m}{M}, \quad m_0 = \frac{M}{N_A}$$

Kinetic Theory of Gases — Assumptions of the Ideal Gas

  1. Random molecular motion
  2. Negligible intermolecular forces (except in collisions)
  3. Molecule volume is negligible compared to container volume
  4. Molecules are identical, hard spheres
  5. All collisions (between molecules and with walls) are perfectly elastic
  6. Between collisions, molecules move in straight lines at constant speed
  7. Newtonian mechanics applies

Kinetic Theory Equation

$$pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm_0\overline{v^2}$$

From which $p = \tfrac{1}{3}\rho \overline{v^2}$ where $\rho = Nm_0/V$.

Cause of Gas Pressure

Molecules collide with container walls. Each collision changes the molecule's momentum, exerting a force (Newton's 2nd/3rd law). The average force per unit area is the pressure. Pressure depends on:

  • Average molecular speed (kinetic energy)
  • Number of molecules striking unit area per unit time

Ideal Gas Equation

$$pV = nRT = NkT$$

$R = 8.31$ J/(mol·K), $k = R/N_A = 1.38 \times 10^{-23}$ J/K (Boltzmann constant).

Connection: Temperature and Kinetic Energy

Derivation

Equate $pV = \tfrac{1}{3}Nm_0\overline{v^2}$ and $pV = NkT$:

$\tfrac{1}{3}m_0\overline{v^2} = kT \Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{2}m_0\overline{v^2} = \tfrac{3}{2}kT$

$$\langle E_K \rangle = \frac{3kT}{2}, \quad v_{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{v^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m_0}}$$
Temperature is a measure of the average translational kinetic energy of molecules.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

Total pressure of a non-reacting gas mixture = sum of partial pressures: $p = p_1 + p_2 + \cdots + p_n$.

Absolute Zero (T = 0 K)

The lowest possible temperature. At absolute zero, kinetic energy of atoms/molecules is minimum (zero in classical view) and internal energy is minimum.

09Thermodynamics

Energy transfers, heat engines, and the fundamental directionality of nature (entropy).

Internal Energy

Internal energy U = sum of kinetic and potential energies of all molecules.

For an ideal gas: intermolecular forces = 0 → no PE. Internal energy depends only on temperature:

$$U_{\text{monoatomic}} = \tfrac{3}{2}nRT$$ $$U_{\text{diatomic}} = \tfrac{5}{2}nRT$$ $$U_{\text{polyatomic}} = \tfrac{6}{2}nRT$$

For monoatomic ideal gas: $\Delta U = \tfrac{3}{2}nR\Delta T$.

Work Done by/on a Gas (Isobaric)

$$W_{by\, gas} = p\Delta V$$

On a p–V diagram, the area under the curve = magnitude of work done.

First Law of Thermodynamics

Increase in internal energy = heat supplied to system + work done on system.
$$\Delta U = Q + W_{on}$$
  • Gas expands → work done on system is negative
  • Gas compressed → work done on system is positive
  • Heat added → $Q > 0$; heat removed → $Q < 0$

Adiabatic compression: temperature rises (gas heating in diesel engines). Adiabatic expansion: temperature falls.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

Several equivalent statements:

  • It is impossible to convert thermal energy completely into work (η < 100%)
  • Heat does not flow spontaneously from cold to hot
  • The entropy of an isolated system never decreases: $\Delta S \geq 0$

Heat Engines

A heat engine takes heat from a hot reservoir, converts part to work, dumps the rest to a cold reservoir.

$$W = Q_H - Q_C, \quad \eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{Q_H - Q_C}{Q_H}$$

Carnot Engine (max efficiency)

An idealized reversible engine. Carnot cycle: 2 isothermals + 2 adiabatics. Maximum possible efficiency between two reservoirs:

$$\eta_{Carnot} = \frac{T_H - T_C}{T_H}$$

$\eta_{real} < \eta_{Carnot}$ because real engines have friction, heat loss, and irreversible processes.

Entropy

Entropy $S$ is a measure of disorder. Change in entropy: $\Delta S = Q/T$.
  • Entropy ↑ when heat added to system
  • Reversible processes: $\Delta S = 0$. Real processes: $\Delta S > 0$
  • Natural processes move toward greater disorder
  • For non-isolated system: $\Delta S_{system} + \Delta S_{environment} \geq 0$

Vapours & Surface Tension (briefly)

Saturated vapour: vapour in equilibrium with its liquid. Saturated vapour pressure (SVP) increases with T but is independent of V (because more vapour can simply condense if compressed). Doesn't obey gas laws.

Surface tension $\sigma = F/l$ — tangential force per unit length in a liquid surface. Decreases with temperature.

10Geometrical Optics

Light traced as rays — useful when wavelength is much smaller than apparatus.

Spherical Mirrors — Definitions

  • C (centre of curvature): centre of the sphere
  • P (pole): centre of the mirror surface
  • F (focal point): where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex)
  • r: radius of curvature; $f = r/2$

Real vs Virtual Images

Real image: light rays actually converge there; can be projected on a screen.
Virtual image: rays only appear to come from that point; cannot be projected on a screen.

Mirror & Lens Equation

$$\frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}$$

Sign conventions:

  • $d_o$: positive for real object (in front of lens/mirror)
  • $d_i$: positive for real image (in front of mirror; opposite side for lens)
  • $f$: positive for converging (concave mirror, convex lens); negative for diverging

Magnification

$$m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o}$$
  • $|m| > 1$: image larger than object
  • $m > 0$: image upright; $m < 0$: image inverted

Wavefronts & Huygens' Principle

Wavefront: surface where all points have the same phase. Ray: direction of wave travel, perpendicular to wavefront.
Huygens' principle: Every point on a wavefront acts as a source of secondary wavelets. The new wavefront is the tangent envelope of all wavelets.

Snell's Law (Refraction)

$$n_1\sin\theta_1 = n_2\sin\theta_2, \quad n = \frac{c}{v}, \quad \frac{\sin\theta_1}{\sin\theta_2} = \frac{v_1}{v_2} = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$

Total Internal Reflection

When light goes from denser to less dense medium ($n_1 > n_2$), and angle of incidence exceeds critical angle:

$$\sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$$

Used in: optic fibres, prismatic binoculars, periscopes.

Refraction Through a Plane-Parallel Plate

Emerging ray is parallel to incident ray but laterally shifted by:

$$x = \frac{d \sin(i_1 - r)}{\cos r}$$

Prism: Deviation & Dispersion

White light entering a prism is split because $n$ depends on wavelength: violet refracts more than red. This is dispersion.

Lenses

Lens maker's equation:

$$\frac{1}{f} = \left(\frac{n_L}{n_M}-1\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$$

Power of a Lens

$$P = \frac{1}{f}$$

Units: dioptre (m⁻¹). Converging lens: P > 0. Diverging: P < 0.

Two-Lens Systems

Image of first lens = object for second lens. If image of lens 1 is behind lens 2, it's a virtual object for lens 2 ($d_o < 0$). Total magnification: $m = m_1 \cdot m_2$.

Lens Aberrations

Spherical aberration: outer rays focus closer than central rays → blurred image. Fix: aperture/diaphragm to block outer rays, or aspheric lens design.
Chromatic aberration: different wavelengths focus at different points (violet closer than red). Fix: achromatic doublet — convex crown glass + concave flint glass — corrects two wavelengths to same focus.

11Waves

Disturbances that transport energy without transporting matter.

Mechanical vs Electromagnetic Waves

Mechanical waves require a medium (water waves, sound, waves on a string). They are oscillations propagating through an elastic medium.
Electromagnetic waves need no medium (radio, light, X-rays, γ). All travel at $c = 3\times 10^8$ m/s in vacuum.

Wave Properties

  • Progressive wave: carries energy from one point to another
  • Wavelength (λ): distance between adjacent points in phase
  • Frequency (f): oscillations per second; $f = 1/T$
  • Amplitude (A): maximum displacement from equilibrium
  • Phase difference: two points one λ apart differ by 0° (in phase)
  • Intensity: $I \propto A^2$ (power per unit area)

Wave Equation

$$v = f\lambda$$

Transverse vs Longitudinal

  • Transverse: oscillation perpendicular to wave direction (all EM waves, waves on a string)
  • Longitudinal: oscillation parallel to wave direction (sound waves)

Diffraction

Spreading of waves when passing through a gap or around an obstacle.

Diffraction is significant when gap width is comparable to wavelength. Larger gap → less spread; shorter wavelength → less spread.

12Stationary Waves

When two equal waves traveling in opposite directions superpose, a stationary wave forms.

Formation

A stationary (standing) wave forms from superposition of two waves with same speed, frequency, and amplitude traveling in opposite directions.

Typically formed by reflection of a progressive wave at a boundary.

Nodes and Antinodes

  • Node: point of zero displacement (always, at all times)
  • Antinode: point of maximum amplitude
  • Adjacent nodes are λ/2 apart; same for adjacent antinodes
  • Node-to-antinode distance = λ/4

Strings (Both Ends Fixed)

Both ends are nodes. Allowed wavelengths: $\lambda_n = 2L/n$ ($n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$).

$$f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}$$

$f_1$ = fundamental (first harmonic); $f_2 = 2f_1$ (second harmonic); etc. All harmonics present.

Air Columns — One End Closed

Closed end is a displacement node, open end is an antinode. Allowed wavelengths: $\lambda_n = 4L/n$ ($n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots$ — only odd).

$$f_n = \frac{nv}{4L} \quad (n = 1, 3, 5, \ldots)$$

Air Columns — Both Ends Open

Both ends are antinodes. Allowed wavelengths: $\lambda_n = 2L/n$ ($n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$).

$$f_n = \frac{nv}{2L} \quad (n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots)$$

Measuring Wavelength of Sound (Kundt's Tube)

A loudspeaker drives a tube with fine powder. At nodes (zero air motion), powder accumulates; at antinodes (max motion) it's blown away. Measure node-to-node distance = λ/2.

End Correction (Eliminating Errors)

The antinode at an open tube end is slightly beyond the tube. Using two resonance lengths ($l_1$ and $l_2$) eliminates this systematic error: $\lambda = 2(l_2 - l_1)$.

13Interference & Diffraction

When waves meet, their displacements add — leading to interference patterns that reveal the wave nature of light.

Principle of Superposition

When two or more waves meet at a point, the resultant displacement equals the algebraic (vector) sum of the individual displacements.

Coherence

Coherent waves: same frequency AND constant phase difference. Required to produce a stable interference pattern.

Two waves can interfere if: same type, same direction of polarization, meet at the same point.

Constructive vs Destructive Interference

  • Constructive: path difference = $n\lambda$ (waves arrive in phase, add up)
  • Destructive: path difference = $(n + \tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$ (waves arrive out of phase, cancel)

Young's Double-Slit Experiment

Thomas Young (1801) showed light is a wave by demonstrating two-slit interference.

$$\lambda = \frac{ax}{D}$$

Where $a$ = slit separation, $x$ = fringe spacing, $D$ = distance from slits to screen. (Uses small-angle approximation: $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta$.)

Why this formula

For first-order constructive: $a\sin\theta = \lambda$. Fringe position on screen: $x = D\tan\theta$. Small angles: $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta$, so $\lambda/x = a/D$ → $\lambda = ax/D$.

Diffraction Grating

Many parallel slits with equal spacing $d$ (grating spacing or grating element).

$$d\sin\theta = n\lambda \quad (n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots)$$

$n$ = order of maximum. Why gratings beat double-slits:

  • Bright maxima are much sharper (intensity scales as $N^2$)
  • Wider angular separation → more accurate measurements
  • $d$ can be measured more precisely (lines per mm)

White Light Through a Grating

Each order produces a continuous spectrum. From $d\sin\theta = n\lambda$: longer wavelength → larger angle. Red diffracts more than violet (opposite to a prism!).

14Electromagnetic Oscillations & Waves

Charge, current, and field oscillations — the source and nature of all EM radiation.

Free EM Oscillations (LC Circuit)

An ideal LC circuit (no resistance) oscillates between energy stored in capacitor (E-field) and inductor (B-field).

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{LC}, \quad \omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}, \quad f = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$$

Charge and current oscillate sinusoidally:

$$q = q_{max}\cos\omega t, \quad I = -I_{max}\sin\omega t, \quad I_{max} = \omega q_{max}$$

Energy Conservation in LC Circuit

$$E_{total} = \frac{q^2}{2C} + \frac{LI^2}{2} = \frac{q_{max}^2}{2C} = \frac{LI_{max}^2}{2} = \text{const}$$

Forced EM Oscillations

An RLC circuit driven by an external EMF $\varepsilon = \varepsilon_0 \cos\omega t$ undergoes forced oscillations. Resonance occurs at $\omega = 1/\sqrt{LC}$.

Nature of EM Waves

  • Produced by accelerating charges
  • Consist of changing E and B fields, perpendicular to each other AND to direction of travel: $\vec{E} \perp \vec{B} \perp \vec{c}$
  • Transverse waves
  • E and B fields oscillate in phase
  • Travel through vacuum at $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s
  • Different EM waves differ only in frequency/wavelength

15Alternating Current

Sinusoidally varying voltage and current — the basis of power grids worldwide.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

The induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage.
$$\varepsilon = -N\frac{\Delta\Phi}{\Delta t}$$

Lenz's Law

The direction of induced current opposes the change in magnetic flux that produces it. (Conservation of energy.)

AC Generator

A coil rotates in a magnetic field. As it rotates, flux through it changes:

$$\Phi = BA\cos\omega t \Rightarrow \varepsilon = NBA\omega\sin\omega t = \varepsilon_0\sin\omega t$$

$\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega$ — peak EMF.

AC Equations

$$I = I_0\sin\omega t, \quad V = V_0\sin\omega t$$

Period $T = 1/f = 2\pi/\omega$.

RMS (Root Mean Square)

RMS value of AC = the DC value that would produce the same average power in a resistor.
$$I_{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}$$

Why √2

Average of $\sin^2\omega t$ over a cycle = 1/2. So $\langle P\rangle = \langle I^2 R\rangle = I_0^2 R/2 = (I_0/\sqrt{2})^2 R$. Thus $I_{rms} = I_0/\sqrt{2}$.

Average AC Power

$$\langle P\rangle = I_{rms}V_{rms} = \frac{I_0V_0}{2}$$

Transformers

Two coils on a soft iron core. AC in primary creates changing flux in core, which induces EMF in secondary.

$$\frac{V_S}{V_P} = \frac{N_S}{N_P}, \quad I_PV_P = I_SV_S \text{ (ideal)}$$

Step-up: $N_S > N_P$ → higher voltage, lower current. Step-down: opposite.

Transformer Design Choices

  • Iron core (ferromagnetic): improves flux linkage
  • Continuous loop: minimizes flux loss
  • Laminated core: reduces eddy currents → less heat loss
  • Low-resistance copper coils: reduces $I^2R$ losses

Why High-Voltage Power Transmission

$$P_{loss} = \frac{P_{station}^2}{V^2}R$$

Doubling V → losses ÷ 4. Quintupling V → losses ÷ 25. Massive economic savings — fewer power plants needed.

Rectification

Conversion of AC to DC using diodes (which conduct in one direction only).

  • Half-wave: single diode; only positive half-cycles pass; output is on for half the time
  • Full-wave (bridge): 4 diodes; both half-cycles produce output in same direction; doubles efficiency

Smoothing

Capacitor in parallel with load smooths the rectified output. Time constant $\tau = RC$ should be much larger than the AC period.

Capacitor charges during voltage peaks, discharges through load between peaks → output has small "ripple" rather than going to zero.

16Charged Particles

How electrons and ions move in fields — basis of CRTs, mass spectrometers, and particle accelerators.

Millikan's Oil Drop Experiment

Goal: determine the elementary charge $e$.

Procedure:

  1. Spray oil drops between two horizontal parallel plates (drops charge by friction or X-ray ionization)
  2. Without field: drop falls; measure terminal velocity $v$ → use Stokes' law to find mass $m$
  3. Apply electric field $E = V/d$; adjust until drop stationary: $mg = qE = qV/d$
  4. Calculate $q = mgd/V$
Result: Charge is quantized — always an integer multiple of $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C.

Charged Particle in a Uniform Electric Field

Force is constant: $F = qE$. Acceleration: $a = qE/m$.

If charge enters perpendicular to field with velocity $v$:

$$y = \frac{1}{2}\frac{qE}{mv^2}x^2$$

Path is parabolic (analogous to projectile motion). Kinetic energy changes (work is done by the field).

Charged Particle in a Uniform Magnetic Field

Magnetic force on a moving charge:

$$F = qvB\sin\alpha$$

α = angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$. Direction by Fleming's left-hand rule (for positive charge).

Magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity → does no work → kinetic energy unchanged.

Circular Motion in a Magnetic Field

If $\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$, the magnetic force provides centripetal force:

$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \Rightarrow r = \frac{mv}{qB}, \quad T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$$
  • Faster particles → larger circles ($r \propto v$)
  • Heavier particles → larger circles ($r \propto m$)
  • Stronger field → tighter circles ($r \propto 1/B$)
  • If velocity not perpendicular: helical path

Crossed E and B Fields (Velocity Selector)

If E and B are perpendicular and produce opposing forces on a charge, only particles with the right velocity pass straight through:

$$qE = qvB \Rightarrow v = \frac{E}{B}$$

Used in mass spectrometers to filter beams by velocity, regardless of charge or mass.

Specific Charge of the Electron (e/m)

Electrons accelerated through PD $V_a$ acquire velocity $v$ from $eV_a = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$, so:

$$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{v^2}{2V_a}$$

With crossed fields ($v = E/B$):

$$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{E^2}{2V_a B^2}$$

17Quantum Physics

The breakdown of classical physics at atomic scales — and the bizarre new rules that took its place. Likely a major МЭСК focus.

Wave Theory of Light (classical)

By the late 19th century, light was firmly believed to be a wave because it shows reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, and polarization. Maxwell showed light is an electromagnetic wave. But then...

The Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric effect: emission of electrons from a metal surface when EM radiation hits it.

Experimental observations (which classical wave theory could NOT explain):

  1. Emission is instantaneous — no delay even at low intensity
  2. Below a threshold frequency $f_0$, no emission occurs, regardless of intensity or duration
  3. Above $f_0$, increasing intensity increases the number of electrons but NOT their KE
  4. Increasing frequency increases the maximum KE of emitted electrons
  5. Different metals have different threshold frequencies
  6. Each photon interacts with one electron (1-to-1)
Why classical wave theory fails:
  • Predicts: any frequency works if intense enough or long enough — wrong
  • Predicts: intensity increases KE — wrong
  • Predicts: time delay needed for low-intensity light — wrong

Photon Theory (Planck & Einstein)

Planck (1901): EM energy comes in discrete packets (quanta). Einstein (1905): these packets persist as photons.

$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}, \quad h = 6.63\times 10^{-34} \text{ J·s}$$

1 eV = $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J = energy gained by an electron through 1 V.

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

One photon transfers ALL its energy to one electron. If frequency too low, no electron escapes. Excess energy becomes the electron's kinetic energy:

$$hf = \Phi + \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$$
  • Work function $\Phi$: minimum energy to escape the metal surface
  • Threshold frequency: $f_0 = \Phi/h$
  • Stopping potential: $eV_s = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$
$$hf = hf_0 + eV_s$$

Plotting KE vs Frequency

A graph of $\tfrac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2$ vs $f$ gives:

  • Straight line with gradient = h (Planck's constant)
  • X-intercept = $f_0$ (threshold frequency)
  • Y-intercept = $-\Phi$ (work function)

Wave-Particle Duality of Light

Wave evidence: diffraction, interference, polarization. Particle evidence: photoelectric effect, Compton effect.

Light propagates as a wave but interacts with matter as a particle (photon).

De Broglie Hypothesis (1924)

All moving particles have an associated wave. The wavelength is given by
$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$

For an electron accelerated through PD $V$: $eV = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2eV/m}$, so:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{\sqrt{2meV}}$$

Davisson-Germer Experiment (1927)

Confirmed de Broglie. Beam of electrons scattered from a nickel crystal showed diffraction maxima at angles predicted by treating electrons as waves with wavelength $\lambda = h/p$.

For electron diffraction by graphite: $r \propto \lambda \propto 1/\sqrt{V}$ (ring radius decreases as accelerating voltage increases).

Why Classical Physics Fails for Atoms

An orbiting electron is constantly accelerating. By Maxwell's equations, accelerated charges radiate EM waves and lose energy. So the electron should spiral into the nucleus in nanoseconds. Atoms shouldn't exist. They do. Classical physics is wrong at atomic scale.

Bohr's Postulates (1913)

Postulate 1: Electrons exist only in certain fixed orbits (stationary states) where they don't radiate. Angular momentum is quantized:

$$mvr = n\frac{h}{2\pi}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

Postulate 2: When an electron transitions from higher to lower orbit, the energy difference is emitted as a photon:

$$hf = E_2 - E_1$$

Hydrogen Energy Levels

Combining Bohr's postulates with Coulomb's law and centripetal force:

$$E_n = -\frac{me^4}{8\varepsilon_0^2 h^2}\cdot\frac{1}{n^2} = -\frac{13.6 \text{ eV}}{n^2}$$

$E_1 = -13.6$ eV (ground state), $E_2 = -3.4$ eV, $E_3 = -1.51$ eV, ..., $E_\infty = 0$.

  • Energies are quantized (only specific values allowed)
  • Ground state: $n = 1$, lowest energy, atom is stable
  • Excited state: electron in higher level (atom unstable, returns spontaneously)
  • Negative because we set zero at infinity (electron unbound)

Why line spectra exist: electron transitions between discrete energy levels emit/absorb photons at specific frequencies $f = (E_2 - E_1)/h$. Each line corresponds to one transition. Line spectra are direct evidence of discrete energy levels in atoms.

Successes & Failures of Bohr's Model

Successes: Explained hydrogen spectrum exactly; explained atomic stability; worked for one-electron ions (He⁺).

Failures: Couldn't explain multi-electron atoms; couldn't predict line intensities; couldn't account for fine structure (each level splits into closely-spaced sub-levels).

Band Theory of Solids

When atoms come together in a crystal, individual energy levels broaden into bands:

  • Valence band: uppermost filled (or partly filled) band
  • Conduction band: lowest unfilled (or partly filled) band
  • Forbidden band (band gap): energies not allowed for electrons

18Nuclear Physics

The structure of the atomic nucleus, radioactive decay, and the energy released by nuclear reactions.

Rutherford's α-Scattering (1911)

Geiger and Marsden fired α-particles at thin gold foil. Most passed straight through; some deflected; a tiny fraction (~1 in 8000) bounced back at >90°.

Conclusions:

  • Most of the atom is empty space
  • Mass and positive charge concentrated in a tiny nucleus (~10⁻¹⁵ m, 99.9% of mass)
  • Electrons orbit the nucleus (analogy to solar system)

Spontaneous & Random Nature of Decay

  • Spontaneous: not affected by chemical reactions, T, P, or other nuclei
  • Random: impossible to predict which nucleus decays when; each nucleus has the same probability per unit time

Activity, Decay Constant, Half-Life

Decay constant λ: probability of decay per unit time. Property of the nuclide.
Activity A: number of decays per second. Unit: becquerel (Bq).
$$A = \lambda N$$

Number of undecayed nuclei follows exponential decay:

$$N = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}, \quad A = A_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$
Half-life $t_{1/2}$: time for half the nuclei to decay.
$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}$$

Background Radiation Correction

Measured count rate $R$ from a sample is generally less than activity (detector misses most decays). Always subtract background count rate before analysis.

Mass-Energy Equivalence

$$E = mc^2$$

Energy equivalent of 1 u: 931 MeV. Tiny mass changes correspond to huge energies on nuclear scales.

Mass Defect & Binding Energy

Mass defect $\Delta m$ = (mass of separated nucleons) − (mass of nucleus). Always positive.
Binding energy $E_B = \Delta m c^2$ — the energy required to separate all nucleons from the nucleus to infinity.

Binding Energy Per Nucleon

Best measure of nuclear stability. Plot of BE/nucleon vs A:

  • Rises steeply for light nuclei
  • Peaks near $^{56}$Fe (~8.8 MeV/nucleon) — most stable
  • Falls slowly for heavy nuclei

Light nuclei + Light nuclei → bigger: moves up the curve → fusion releases energy

Heavy nucleus → two medium nuclei: moves up the curve → fission releases energy

Nuclear Fission

A heavy nucleus splits into two lighter ones, releasing neutrons and energy. Example:

$$^{235}_{\,92}\text{U} + n \to {}^{141}_{\,56}\text{Ba} + {}^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3n + \text{energy}$$

Chain Reaction & Reactors

Neutrons released in one fission trigger further fissions → exponential chain. Atomic bomb: uncontrolled chain reaction. Reactor: controlled.

Reactor components:

  • Fuel rods: uranium-235 (or plutonium)
  • Moderator (water, graphite, heavy water): slows down neutrons so they can cause more fission
  • Control rods (cadmium, boron, hafnium): absorb neutrons; raised/lowered to control reaction rate
  • Heat exchanger: transfers thermal energy to water → steam → turbines → electricity

Nuclear Fusion

Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier one. Example:

$$^2_1\text{H} + {}^2_1\text{H} \to {}^3_2\text{He} + n + \text{energy}$$

Requires extreme T (10⁷–10⁸ K) and P to overcome electrostatic repulsion. Source of stellar energy via the proton-proton chain.

Stellar Fusion

In the Sun, four protons effectively become a helium-4 nucleus (with positrons, neutrinos, and gamma rays released). Heavier elements (up to Fe) form in larger stars. Elements heavier than Fe form in supernovas.

3. Импульс + Гравитация (Q3 — каждый год, ~11 баллов)

$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$  (угловая скорость через период)
$v_{orbit} = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$  (вывод: $\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$)
$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM}$  (3-й закон Кеплера — вывести)
$v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{r}}$ (2-я космическая скорость)
Почему $E_p = mgh$ не работает для спутников: g не постоянно, поле неоднородно на больших расстояниях
Свойства геостац. орбиты: T=24 ч, над экватором, неподвижен относительно Земли, ~42000 км от центра

Что просят: (a) объяснить ускорение ракеты через импульс газов/3-й закон Ньютона [2], (b) найти радиус орбиты из GMm/r²=mω²r → r=(GMT²/4π²)^(1/3)≈42000 км [4], (c) E_p=-GMm/r≈-1.91×10⁹ Дж [1], (d) почему mgh не подходит [2].
Ключ: Приравниваете гравитационную силу к центростремительной. Вывод по цепочке: ω→v→r или сразу r из T.

4. Газы + Термодинамика (Q4 — каждый год, ~10 баллов)

Что просят: (a) показать согласованность через V₁/T₁=V₂/T₂ [2], (b) свойства идеального газа [2], (c) W=pΔV=100×2×10⁻⁶=0.20 Дж [2], ΔU=Q-W=0.50 Дж [2], (d) в реальности Q больше из-за теплопотерь [2].

5. Переменный ток / RLC (Q5 — каждый год, ~10 баллов)

$x = x_{max}\sin\omega t$ (ток/напряжение)
$\omega = 2\pi f$  (КРИТИЧНО — нужно для всех расчётов!)
$X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C}$  (ёмкостное реактивное сопротивление)
$X_L = \omega L$  (индуктивное реактивное сопротивление)
$Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2}$  (импеданс)
$I_{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}$,   $V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}$  (действующие значения)
$\langle P\rangle = I_{rms}V_{rms} = \frac{I_0 V_0}{2}$  (средняя мощность)
Резонанс: $X_L = X_C$ → $\omega = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$ → Z минимален, I максимален
Фазорная диаграмма: R горизонтально, X_L вверх, X_C вниз. Если X_C>X_L — результирующий вектор вниз.
Типичная задача (2025 Q5): R=40 Ω, L=0.40 H, C=17 μF, V₀=25 В, f=50 Гц.
Решение: ω=2π×50=314 рад/с. X_C=1/(314×17×10⁻⁶)≈187 Ω. X_L=314×0.40≈126 Ω. Z=√(40²+(126-187)²)=√(1600+3721)≈73 Ω. I_rms=(25/√2)/73≈0.24 А.
Ключ: Не забывай делить V₀ на √2 для I_rms! Фазор: X_C>X_L → ток опережает напряжение.

6. Трансформатор + Выпрямление (Q6 — каждый год, ~10 баллов)

$\frac{N_s}{N_p} = \frac{V_s}{V_p}$  (коэффициент трансформации)
$V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}$ — нужно для перевода пикового ↔ действующего
$P_{loss} = I^2R = \frac{P^2}{V^2}R$ — потери при передаче энергии
Мостовой выпрямитель: 4 диода, full-wave. Конденсатор параллельно нагрузке для сглаживания.
Если диод сгорел → full-wave → half-wave (период удваивается)
Больше R → больше τ=RC → меньше пульсации (более гладкий выход)
Типичная задача (2025 Q6): Трансформатор: N_s=200, вход 220 В rms, выход 12 В пиковое.
Решение: V₁_rms=220, V₂_peak=12 → V₂_rms=12/√2≈8.49. N_p/N_s=V_p/V_s → N_p=200×220/8.49≈5185.
Также просят: нарисовать 4 диода (A положительный), добавить конденсатор, описать эффект увеличения R и поломки диода.

7. Оптика (Q7 — каждый год, ~8 баллов)

$\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{u} + \frac{1}{v}$,   $\frac{1}{f} = \left(\frac{n - n_0}{n_0}\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)$
$n_1\sin\theta_1 = n_2\sin\theta_2$  (закон Снелля — НЕ дают!)
$\sin\theta_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$  (критический угол)
$m = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{v}{u}$  (увеличение)
Сферическая аберрация: лучи на краю линзы фокусируются ближе → размытие. Хроматическая: разные длины волн → разные фокусы.
В воде: (n_lens - n_water)/n_water меньше чем (n_lens - 1)/1 → f увеличивается
Типичная задача (2025 Q7): Плосковыпуклая линза, n=1.5, R=20 см.
Решение: 1/f=(1.5-1)(1/0.20-0)=0.5/0.20=2.5 → f=40 см. В воде f больше (объяснить через формулу линзмейкера). Аберрация — сферическая (не хроматическая, т.к. n одинаков для всех λ).

8. Заряженные частицы (Q8 — каждый год, ~11 баллов)

$F = qE$  (сила в электрическом поле)
$F = qvB\sin\alpha$  (сила Лоренца — НЕ дают!)
$v = \frac{E}{B}$  (селектор скоростей: qE = qvB)
$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$  (радиус в магн. поле: qvB = mv²/r)
$\frac{e}{m} = \frac{v}{Br}$  (удельный заряд)
$eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$  (ускорение через разность потенциалов)
Правило левой руки Флеминга (для + заряда). Для электрона — направление силы противоположное.
Магнитная сила НЕ совершает работу (⊥ скорости) → KE не меняется
Позитрон: та же масса что у электрона → тот же радиус, но отклоняется в ПРОТИВОПОЛОЖНУЮ сторону
Типичная задача (2025 Q8): Селектор: B₁=6.00 мТл, E=300 кВ/м. Затем B₂=40.0 мТл (в страницу).
Решение: v=E/B=300000/0.006=5×10⁷ м/с. В B₂: r=mv/(eB)=9.11×10⁻³¹×5×10⁷/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹×0.04)≈0.028 м.
Также: направление B₁ — из страницы (left-hand rule: электрон летит вправо, E-сила вниз, B-сила должна быть вверх). Позитрон: тот же радиус, противоположная траектория.

9. Фотоэффект (Q9 — каждый год, ~10 баллов)

$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$  (энергия фотона — НЕ дают!)
$hf = \Phi + \frac{1}{2}mv^2_{max}$  (уравнение Эйнштейна — НЕ дают!)
$\Phi = hf_0$  (работа выхода через пороговую частоту)
$eV_s = \frac{1}{2}mv^2_{max}$  (задерживающий потенциал)
$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$  (длина волны де Бройля)
Определение work function: minimum photon energy required to eject an electron from the metal surface
3 доказательства корпускулярной природы: (1) KE зависит от частоты, не интенсивности; (2) число электронов зависит от интенсивности; (3) нет временной задержки; (4) один фотон — один электрон
Типичная задача (2025 Q9): График E_max(eV) vs f(×10¹⁴ Гц). Найти f₀, Φ, нарисовать линию для другого металла.
Решение: f₀ считывается с графика (где линия пересекает ось f) ≈ 5.8×10¹⁴ Гц. Φ=hf₀=6.63×10⁻³⁴×5.8×10¹⁴≈3.8×10⁻¹⁹ Дж. Другой металл с меньшей Φ: параллельная линия ЛЕВЕЕ (тот же наклон h, но меньший x-intercept).
Ключ: Наклон графика E_max vs f всегда = h (постоянная Планка). Для разных металлов линии параллельны!

10. Ядерная физика (Q10 — каждый год, ~9 баллов)

$E = mc^2$,   $\Delta E = \Delta m \cdot c^2$ (или $\Delta m \times 931$ МэВ/u)
Определение half-life: the time taken for the number of undecayed nuclei to reduce by half
Кварковый состав: p = uud (+2/3+2/3-1/3 = +1), n = udd (+2/3-1/3-1/3 = 0)
β⁻ распад: n→p+e⁻+ν̄ (Z увеличивается на 1, A не меняется). На уровне кварков: d→u+W⁻
Подсчёт кварков в ядре: каждый протон = 2u+1d, каждый нейтрон = 1u+2d
Свойства α, β, γ: ионизация (α>β>γ), проникающая способность (γ>β>α), отклонение в полях

11. Физические величины и измерения (может быть частью любого вопроса)

Нет специальных формул в справочнике для этой темы
Погрешности: $\Delta x = \frac{x_{max} - x_{min}}{2}$,   $\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta x}{x_{mean}} \times 100\%$
Сложение/вычитание → складываем абсолютные погрешности. Умножение/деление → складываем процентные.
Степень $x^n$ → процентная погрешность × n
Линеаризация: $y = ax^n$ → $\ln y$ vs $\ln x$ (наклон = n). $y = ae^{kx}$ → $\ln y$ vs $x$ (наклон = k)
7 базовых единиц SI: m, kg, s, A, K, mol, cd
Систематическая ошибка → accuracy↓ (нулевая ошибка). Случайная → precision↓ (параллакс, реакция)
Типичная задача: По графику определить наклон, пересечение с осью Y, вычислить погрешность наклона. Или: проверить однородность формулы по единицам.
Пример: Показать, что $v = \sqrt{2gh}$ однородна: $[v] = \text{m/s}$, $[\sqrt{2gh}] = \sqrt{\text{m/s}^2 \cdot \text{m}} = \text{m/s}$ ✓
Ключ: На экзамене часто просят: (1) нарисовать линию наилучшего соответствия, (2) определить наклон + пересечение, (3) вычислить физ. величину из наклона.

12. Деформация твёрдых тел (~8-10 баллов, был в прошлых годах)

Нет формул деформации в справочнике!
$F = k\Delta l$  (закон Гука, k = жёсткость пружины)
$\sigma = \frac{F}{A}$ (напряжение, Па),   $\varepsilon = \frac{\Delta l}{l_0}$ (относительная деформация, безразм.)
$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{Fl_0}{A\Delta l}$  (модуль Юнга — свойство МАТЕРИАЛА, не объекта)
$E_{упр} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta l)^2 = \frac{1}{2}F\Delta l$  (энергия = площадь под F-x графиком)
Определения: Elastic = возвращается в исходную форму. Plastic = остаётся деформированным. Brittle = ломается без пластичности. Ductile = можно вытянуть в проволоку.
Предел пропорциональности P → предел упругости E → пластическая деформация → разрушение
Типичная задача: Проволока длиной 2.0 м, диаметр 0.5 мм, под нагрузкой 50 Н удлинилась на 1.2 мм. Найти модуль Юнга.
Решение: A = π(0.25×10⁻³)² = 1.96×10⁻⁷ м². E = Fl₀/(AΔl) = 50×2.0/(1.96×10⁻⁷×1.2×10⁻³) ≈ 4.25×10¹¹ Па.
Также просят: описать эксперимент (микрометр для d, линейка для l₀, отметка+линейка для Δl), нарисовать F-Δl график, показать P и E на графике.

13. Колебания / SHM (~10-12 баллов, был в 2023 Q4)

$a = -\omega^2 x$,   $x = A\sin\omega t$,   $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$,   $T = 2\pi\sqrt{l/g}$
$v = A\omega\cos\omega t$,   $v_{max} = A\omega$  (при x=0)
$a_{max} = A\omega^2$  (при x=±A)
$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$  (полная энергия SHM)

Также просят: нарисовать x-t, v-t, a-t графики, объяснить обмен KE↔PE, описать влияние демпфирования на амплитуду.

15. Стоячие волны (~8-10 баллов)

Нет формул стоячих волн в справочнике
Оба конца закреплены (струна): $f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}$, все гармоники (n=1,2,3...)
Один конец закрыт (труба): $f_n = \frac{nv}{4L}$, только НЕЧЁТНЫЕ гармоники (n=1,3,5...)
Оба конца открыты (труба): $f_n = \frac{nv}{2L}$, все гармоники
Расстояние между соседними узлами = λ/2. Узел→пучность = λ/4
Стоячая vs бегущая: стоячая НЕ переносит энергию, амплитуда зависит от позиции, фаза одинакова между узлами
Стоячая волна = суперпозиция двух бегущих волн с одинаковой частотой, амплитудой и скоростью, но ПРОТИВОПОЛОЖНЫМИ направлениями
Типичная задача: Труба длиной L=0.85 м, открыта с одного конца. Скорость звука 340 м/с. Найти 1-ю и 3-ю гармоники.
Решение: f₁=v/(4L)=340/(4×0.85)=100 Гц. f₃=3f₁=300 Гц (2-й гармоники нет!).
Также просят: нарисовать моду колебаний (узлы/пучности), объяснить концевую поправку (end correction): $\lambda = 2(l_2 - l_1)$ устраняет систематическую ошибку.

16. Интерференция и дифракция (~8-10 баллов)

Нет формул интерференции/дифракции в справочнике
$\lambda = \frac{ax}{D}$  (двойная щель Юнга: a=расстояние между щелями, x=расстояние между полосами, D=расстояние до экрана)
$d\sin\theta = n\lambda$  (дифракционная решётка, n=0,1,2...)
Конструктивная интерференция: разность хода = $n\lambda$. Деструктивная: $(n+\frac{1}{2})\lambda$
Когерентность: одинаковая частота + постоянная разность фаз → стабильная интерференционная картина
Решётка лучше двойной щели: максимумы РЕЗЧЕ (ярче и уже), можно точнее измерить λ
Белый свет через решётку: каждый порядок даёт спектр (красный отклоняется больше — длиннее λ)
Типичная задача: Решётка 600 линий/мм, λ=550 нм. Найти максимальный порядок и углы.
Решение: d=1/600000=1.67×10⁻⁶ м. Макс. порядок: n≤d/λ=1.67×10⁻⁶/550×10⁻⁹=3.03 → n_max=3. Угол 1-го порядка: sinθ=λ/d=0.330 → θ=19.3°.
Также просят: объяснить условия когерентности, почему лазер лучше лампы (монохроматический + когерентный), нарисовать интерференционную картину.

17. ЭМ колебания и связь (~8-10 баллов, был в 2023 Q9)

Нет формул связи/модуляции в справочнике
$T_{LC} = 2\pi\sqrt{LC}$,   $f_{LC} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}$ (LC контур)
$E = \frac{q^2}{2C} + \frac{LI^2}{2}$ (энергия LC: электрическая + магнитная = const)
$\text{dB} = 10\lg\frac{P_2}{P_1}$ (затухание/усиление в децибелах)
AM: амплитуда несущей меняется, частота постоянна. Полоса = 2f_M. Дальность большая, качество ниже.
FM: частота несущей меняется, амплитуда постоянна. Полоса больше, качество выше, дальность ~30 км.
Цифровой сигнал > аналоговый: регенерация без шума, шифрование, коррекция ошибок, совместим с компьютерами
Найквист: частота выборки > 2× макс. частота сигнала
Каналы: провод (500 кГц) < коаксиал (50 МГц) < микроволновая связь (1 ГГц) < оптоволокно (ТГц)
Типичная задача (2023 Q9): AM-сигнал, несущая 1.2 МГц, аудиосигнал до 5 кГц. Найти полосу пропускания, объяснить модуляцию.
Решение: Полоса = 2×5000=10 кГц. Диапазон: 1195-1205 кГц.
Также просят: сравнить AM vs FM, объяснить преимущества цифрового, рассчитать затухание в dB, описать работу оптоволокна (полное внутреннее отражение).

18. Молекулярная физика (отдельные аспекты, ~5-8 баллов)

$p = \frac{NkT}{V}$,   $p = \frac{1}{3}nm\langle v^2\rangle$
$pV = nRT = NkT$  (уравнение идеального газа)
$\frac{1}{2}m_0\langle v^2\rangle = \frac{3}{2}kT$  (связь температуры и КЕ — КЛЮЧЕВАЯ)
$v_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m_0}}$  (среднеквадратичная скорость)
$N = nN_A$,   $m_0 = M/N_A$  (связь молей и частиц)
7 допущений идеального газа: хаотическое движение, нет межмолекулярных сил, объём молекул мал, идентичные твёрдые сферы, упругие столкновения, прямолинейное движение между столкновениями
Причина давления: молекулы ударяют стенки → изменение импульса → сила (2-й закон Ньютона) → давление = F/A
Закон Дальтона: общее давление = сумма парциальных давлений
Типичная задача: Гелий (M=4 г/моль) при T=300 К. Найти v_rms и среднюю КЕ молекулы.
Решение: m₀=4×10⁻³/6.02×10²³=6.64×10⁻²⁷ кг. v_rms=√(3×1.38×10⁻²³×300/6.64×10⁻²⁷)≈1370 м/с. KE=3/2×kT=3/2×1.38×10⁻²³×300=6.21×10⁻²¹ Дж.
Также просят: объяснить почему лёгкие молекулы быстрее (при той же T), вывести связь pV и KE из кинетической теории.

19. Элементарные частицы и СМ (~5-8 баллов, часть ядерных задач)

Нет формул частиц в справочнике
Кварки: u (+2/3), d (−1/3), s (−1/3), c (+2/3), b (−1/3), t (+2/3)
Барионы = 3 кварка: p = uud, n = udd. Мезоны = кварк + антикварк: π⁺ = ud̄
Лептоны: e⁻, μ⁻, τ⁻ + три нейтрино. НЕ участвуют в сильном взаимодействии
4 фундаментальные силы: сильная (глюон), ЭМ (фотон), слабая (W±, Z⁰), гравитация (гравитон?)
β⁻ распад на уровне кварков: $d \to u + W^- \to u + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$
β⁺ распад: $u \to d + W^+ \to d + e^+ + \nu_e$
Законы сохранения: заряд, барионное число, лептонное число — сохраняются во всех реакциях
Антиматерия: каждой частице соответствует античастица с противоположным зарядом (позитрон e⁺, антипротон p̄)
Типичная задача: Определить кварковый состав ядра, подсчитать число u и d кварков. β-распад на кварковом уровне.
Пример: Углерод-14 (Z=6, N=8): число u = 6×2+8×1=20, число d = 6×1+8×2=22. При β⁻ распаде ¹⁴C→¹⁴N: один d→u через W⁻.
Также просят: нарисовать диаграмму Фейнмана для β-распада, проверить сохранение заряда/барионного/лептонного числа в реакции.

Exam Strategy

  1. Read carefully. Watch for "max", "min", "ratio", "change in", "magnitude". Underline.
  2. Convert to SI before any calculation: cm → m, g → kg, MeV → J, etc.
  3. Sketch a diagram for force/motion problems. Free-body diagrams clarify which forces matter.
  4. Sign conventions matter for vectors. Pick a positive direction and stick with it.
  5. Use conservation laws first (energy, momentum, charge). Often shortcut to the answer.
  6. Significant figures: match the data given (usually 2–3 SF). Don't write 9 digits.
  7. Units check on every answer. If units don't work out, the answer is wrong.
  8. RMS, not peak, in AC power calculations.
  9. Don't forget the minus sign in gravitational PE, Lenz's law, SHM (a = −ω²x).
  10. Magnetic force does no work on a moving charge (perpendicular to velocity).
  11. Photoelectric: if $f < f_0$, no emission — period. Don't try to "build up" energy.
  12. Mass defect: always (separated parts) − (combined nucleus). Positive.

Common Patterns

  • Inverse square law ($1/r^2$): gravitational F, electric F, field strengths g and E, intensity
  • Inverse first power ($1/r$): potentials φ (gravitational, negative) and V (electric)
  • Exponential decay: radioactive nuclei N, capacitor discharge, damped oscillation amplitude
  • Sinusoidal: SHM displacement, AC voltage/current, EM wave fields, photon emission frequencies

Common Pitfalls

  • Using degrees in $\sin\omega t$ when ω is in rad/s
  • Mixing up f and ω (factor of 2π!)
  • Confusing nucleon number A with atomic number Z
  • Forgetting that force-extension graph area = elastic PE only for linear (Hookean) springs
  • Treating Bernoulli's equation as if applicable to viscous or compressible flow
  • Forgetting to subtract background count rate in radioactive activity calculations
  • Mixing "frequency" and "angular frequency" in SHM equations
