What you need to know about Paper 3

Paper 3 consists of two major questions. In Q1, you plan an experiment based on a given formula. In Q2, you analyse data, plot a graph, and calculate uncertainties. Both questions follow the same logic year after year — so once you learn the structure, you can score consistently.

Q1 · 15 marks

Planning Experiment

You are given a physics formula with variables and asked to plan a laboratory experiment: diagram, variables, measurements, analysis, and safety.

  • Defining variables — 2 marks
  • Diagram + measurements — ~5 marks
  • Method of analysis (linearization!) — 3 marks
  • Additional details — max 3 marks
  • Safety — 1 mark
  • Limitation + Improvement — 2 marks
Q2 · 15 marks

Data Analysis

You are given a data table and a formula. You need to linearize, calculate values, plot a graph with error bars, draw best fit and worst acceptable lines, find the gradient + intercept with uncertainties, and determine physical quantities.

  • Gradient/intercept expressions — 1 mark
  • Table calculations + uncertainties — 2 marks
  • Plot + error bars — 2 marks
  • Best fit + worst line — 2 marks
  • Gradient ± uncertainty — 2 marks
  • Y-intercept ± uncertainty — 2 marks
  • Physical quantities + units — 2 marks
  • Final calculation — ~2 marks

The single most important skill KEY

Linearization. 90% of the problems boil down to one thing: turning a formula like $y = a x^n$ or $y = a e^{kx}$ into the form $Y = mX + c$ so you get a straight line on a graph. If you can do this for any formula, you automatically earn 5–6 marks in both Q1 and Q2.

On the 03 Linearization tab you will find an interactive trainer and a table covering all standard cases.

How to study effectively

  1. Day 1. Read the Q1 tab and memorize the universal structure (6 categories). Without it, you will struggle on the exam.
  2. Day 2. Work through the linearization table and complete the interactive trainer on tab 03. The goal is to recognize the formula type within 5 seconds.
  3. Day 3. Read the Q2 tab and study every step of data analysis. Pay special attention to: worst acceptable line and uncertainty in the gradient.
  4. Day 4. Solve all problems on the "Practice" tab on your own first, then check against the solutions.
  5. Day 5. Learn the phrases (tab 05) and go through the checklist (tab 06).
  6. On exam day. Open Q1 and write every category from the checklist — even if you are unsure. Partial marks are awarded.

Q1 — Planning the Experiment 15

This is the highest-value question. If you know the mark scheme structure, marks come easily. It is broken into 6 blocks below. Write your answer strictly in this order with subheadings — it makes it easier for the examiner to find each mark point.

① Defining the problem 2 marks

List the variables. This is the first thing the examiner looks for. Do not skip it!

  • Independent variable — what you change (e.g. $x$ — compression of the spring)
  • Dependent variable — what you measure in response (e.g. $d$ — distance travelled)
  • Constants / control variables — what must remain unchanged (mass, surface, temperature, etc.)
Template:
"I will vary [X] as the independent variable and measure [Y] as the dependent variable.
[m, k, μ, ...] will be kept constant throughout the experiment."
② Diagram of apparatus 1 mark

A labelled diagram of the setup. It can be simple, but all key components must be labelled.

Example: spring → block → ruler along the path of motion

What the diagram must include:

  • All instruments labelled (spring, block, ruler, balance, light gate, etc.)
  • Orientation: horizontal / vertical — must be obvious
  • Direction of motion shown with an arrow (if relevant)
  • Any fixtures shown (clamp / G-clamp)
③ Methods of data collection 3–4 marks

Describe exactly how you measure each variable in the formula. Include instrument + method.

④ Method of analysis 3 marks

This is the heart of Q1. Linearize the formula and describe what to plot.

  1. Linearize the formula — rearrange into $Y = mX + c$ form (see tab 03)
  2. State the graph: "Plot a graph of [Y] against [X]"
  3. State the condition for validity: "If a straight line is produced (passing through the origin), the relationship is verified"
  4. Express the target quantity in terms of gradient and/or y-intercept
Example — spring (from 2025 paper):
From $2md\mu g = kx^2$ → $d = \dfrac{k}{2m\mu g} \cdot x^2$

Plot: $d$ against $x^2$ → straight line through the origin
Gradient: $\dfrac{k}{2m\mu g}$
Therefore: $\mu = \dfrac{k}{2mg \cdot \text{gradient}}$
⑤ Additional details max 3 marks

These are "bonus" details — write them to pick up extra marks. The mark scheme accepts any 3 from this list:

  • D1. Specific method of collecting raw data (measurement details)
  • D2. Method of determining additional constants (e.g. $k$ via Hooke's law $F = kx$)
  • D3. Ruler clamped horizontally for measuring distance
  • D4. Set square for perpendicularity — "use a set square to ensure the ruler is at right angles to the wall"
  • D5. Calibrated instrument: "use a calibrated spring scale / Newton meter"
  • D6. Repeat and average: "repeat the experiment 3 times for each value of x and calculate the mean"
  • D7. Video camera + slow motion: "record with a video camera held level with the apparatus and play back frame by frame"
  • D8. Minimize air resistance: "use a high-density block to minimise the effect of air resistance"
  • D9. Spirit level: "use a spirit level to ensure the surface is horizontal"
Strategy: always write at least 4–5 points from this list to guarantee the maximum 3 marks. Extra points do not hurt.
⑥ Safety 1 mark

One specific safety precaution relevant to this experiment is enough.

  • "Wear safety goggles to prevent injury from a detached spring / flying object"
  • "Securely clamp the apparatus to ensure it does not shift or fall"
  • "Keep electronic equipment away from strong magnets"
  • "Place a cushion / soft barrier to stop the trolley safely"
  • "Avoid placing fingers between strong magnets"
Do not write generic phrases like "be careful" — you need a specific precaution for this experiment.

One mark for the limitation, one for the improvement. They must be linked.

Full sample answer for Q1 2025

Formula: $2md\mu g = kx^2$, find $\mu$.

Expand full answer (15 marks)

① Variables

Independent variable: $x$ (compression of spring). Dependent variable: $d$ (maximum distance travelled by the block). Mass $m$ and spring constant $k$ are kept constant.

② Diagram

(see diagram above) Spring fixed horizontally to wall, block in front of spring, ruler clamped horizontally along the path of motion, set square between ruler and wall.

③ Procedure & measurements

  • Measure mass $m$ of block using top-pan balance
  • Measure original length of spring with ruler
  • Compress spring by amount $x$, measure final length, find $x$ as difference
  • Release block, mark final position when at rest
  • Measure $d$ from spring end to block's final position with the clamped ruler
  • Repeat for at least 6 values of $x$, 3 trials each, take mean of $d$

④ Analysis

Rearrange: $d = \dfrac{k}{2m\mu g} x^2$. Plot $d$ against $x^2$. If straight line through origin, relationship is verified. Gradient $= \dfrac{k}{2m\mu g}$, so $\mu = \dfrac{k}{2mg \cdot \text{gradient}}$.

⑤ Additional details

  • Use spirit level to ensure surface is horizontal (D9)
  • Use set square to keep ruler perpendicular to wall (D4)
  • Determine $k$ separately by hanging known masses on the spring and measuring extension (Hooke's law) (D2)
  • Repeat 3 times and take average (D6)

⑥ Safety

Wear safety goggles in case the spring detaches and flies during release.

⑦ Limitation + Improvement

Limitation: the block might not travel in a straight line, making $d$ inaccurate.
Improvement: use a guiding track / channel to constrain the block's motion to a straight path.

Q2 — Data Analysis 15

Q2 is simpler than Q1 because everything is step-by-step: the data is already given, you just calculate. The key is not to rush through uncertainty levels and the worst acceptable line.

  1. Gradient & y-intercept expressions 1 mark

    Express gradient and intercept from the formula. For example, from $\frac{1}{I} = \frac{1}{E}(R + r)$ → $\frac{1}{I} = \frac{1}{E}R + \frac{r}{E}$, so gradient $= \frac{1}{E}$, y-intercept $= \frac{r}{E}$.

  2. Fill in the table 1 mark

    Calculate the new column (e.g. $\frac{1}{I}$). Use 2 or 3 significant figures. Keep them consistent across all rows.

  3. Absolute uncertainties 1 mark

    If the raw data includes uncertainty ($I = 130 \pm 2$ mA), then the uncertainty in $\frac{1}{I}$ is calculated as:

    $$\Delta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{I}\right) = \tfrac{1}{I} \cdot \tfrac{\Delta I}{I} \quad \text{or} \quad \Delta\!\left(\tfrac{1}{I}\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{I_{\min}} - \tfrac{1}{I_{\max}}\right)$$

    Example: $I = 130 \pm 2$ mA → $\frac{1}{I} = 7.69 \, A^{-1}$, $\Delta = \frac{2}{130} \times 7.69 \approx 0.12$ → write $\pm 0.1$.

  4. Plot graph + error bars 2 marks

    6 points plotted accurately (diameter less than half a small grid square). Error bars are symmetric and proportional to $\Delta(1/I)$.

  5. Best fit + worst acceptable line 2 marks

    Best fit: passes through all error bars, with points balanced (roughly equal numbers above and below the line).
    Worst acceptable line: the steepest or shallowest straight line that still passes through all error bars. Label both lines.

  6. Gradient + uncertainty 2 marks
    $$\text{gradient} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

    The two points must be at least half the line length apart. Then:

    $$\Delta(\text{grad}) = |\text{grad}_{\text{best}} - \text{grad}_{\text{worst}}|$$
  7. Y-intercept + uncertainty 2 marks

    Substitute a point from the line into $y = mx + c$ to find $c$. Do the same for the worst line.

    $$\Delta c = |c_{\text{best}} - c_{\text{worst}}|$$
    Do not use a false origin to calculate the intercept in this question — the mark scheme explicitly does not accept it.
  8. Find physical quantities 2 marks

    From the expressions in step 1: $E = \frac{1}{\text{gradient}}$, $r = \text{intercept} \times E = \frac{\text{intercept}}{\text{gradient}}$.
    Do not forget units and powers of ten! Current in mA → remember to multiply by 10⁻³.

  9. Uncertainty in physical quantity 1 mark
    $$\Delta E = \frac{\Delta(\text{gradient})}{\text{gradient}} \cdot E$$

    Or using the max/min method:

    $$\Delta E = E - \frac{1}{\text{grad}_{\max}} \quad \text{or} \quad \Delta E = \frac{1}{\text{grad}_{\min}} - E$$
  10. Final calculation (e.g. predict I) 1 mark

    Substitute the values of $E$ and $r$ you found into the formula: $I = \frac{E}{R + r}$.

When $n$ is unknown — always use logarithms

If the power in the formula is not given explicitly (e.g. "$y$ depends on $x$ as $y = a x^n$, find $n$"), then always plot lg y vs lg x. You get $n$ as the gradient and $\lg a$ as the intercept.

$$y = ax^n \implies \lg y = n \cdot \lg x + \lg a$$

Practice problems

5 typical Q1 problems covering different topics. Try each one yourself first, then expand the solution to check.

① Compound pendulum Oscillations

Problem. A student investigates how the period of small oscillations $T$ of a compound pendulum depends on the distance $L$ from the pivot to the centre of mass. The expected relationship is:

$$T^2 = \dfrac{4\pi^2}{g}\!\left(\dfrac{I_0}{ML} + L\right)$$

where $I_0$ is the moment of inertia about the centre of mass and $M$ is the mass. Plan an experiment to determine $g$ and $I_0$.

Linearization (the key step)

Multiply both sides by $L$:

$$T^2 L = \dfrac{4\pi^2}{g} L^2 + \dfrac{4\pi^2 I_0}{gM}$$

Plot $T^2 L$ against $L^2$:

  • Gradient = $\dfrac{4\pi^2}{g}$ → $g = \dfrac{4\pi^2}{\text{gradient}}$
  • Y-intercept = $\dfrac{4\pi^2 I_0}{gM}$ → $I_0 = \dfrac{\text{intercept} \cdot gM}{4\pi^2}$

Variables

Independent: $L$. Dependent: $T$. Constants: $M$, geometry of pendulum.

Procedure

  • Suspend rigid body at distance $L$ from CoM (use a rod with holes at known positions)
  • Displace by small angle (< 10°), release, time 20 oscillations with stopwatch
  • $T = $ total time / 20 (reduces timing error)
  • Repeat 3 times and average
  • Vary $L$ (at least 6 values), measure each with ruler/caliper from pivot to CoM
  • Find $M$ on top-pan balance

Safety

Securely clamp the support to the bench so the pendulum doesn't fall.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: human reaction time in stopwatch. Imp: use a light gate at the lowest point of swing.

② Stokes' law — viscosity of a liquid Hydrostatics

Problem. A ball of radius $r$ falls through a viscous liquid at terminal velocity:

$$v = \dfrac{2 r^2 (\rho - \sigma) g}{9 \eta}$$

where $\rho$ is the density of the ball, $\sigma$ is the density of the liquid, and $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity. Plan an experiment to determine $\eta$.

Linearization

$v$ against $r^2$ → straight line through the origin, gradient $= \dfrac{2(\rho-\sigma)g}{9\eta}$

$$\eta = \dfrac{2(\rho-\sigma)g}{9 \cdot \text{gradient}}$$

Variables

Independent: $r$ (radius of ball). Dependent: $v$ (terminal velocity). Constants: $\rho$, $\sigma$, temperature (viscosity depends on $T$!).

Procedure

  • Use balls of different radii (steel ball bearings); measure each with micrometer
  • Use long graduated cylinder filled with viscous liquid (glycerine)
  • Mark two lines well below the surface so terminal velocity is reached
  • Drop ball, time travel between marks: $v = $ distance / time
  • Use light gates for accurate timing (no reaction-time error)
  • Repeat 3 times per radius, take mean
  • Find density of ball: $\rho = m/V$, where $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$
  • Find density of liquid with hydrometer / displacement

Safety

Clean up spills immediately to prevent slipping.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: ball may not reach terminal velocity in short cylinder. Imp: use very tall cylinder, place first mark much lower.

③ Photoelectric effect — Planck's constant Quantum physics

Problem. The photoelectric effect is described by:

$$eV_0 = hf - \phi$$

where $V_0$ is the stopping voltage, $f$ is the frequency of incident light, and $\phi$ is the work function. Plan an experiment to determine $h$ and $\phi$.

Linearization

$V_0 = \dfrac{h}{e}f - \dfrac{\phi}{e}$ → plot $V_0$ vs $f$:

  • Gradient = $h/e$ → $h = e \cdot \text{gradient}$
  • Y-intercept = $-\phi/e$ → $\phi = -e \cdot \text{intercept}$

Variables

Independent: $f$ (frequency of incident light). Dependent: $V_0$ (stopping voltage). Constants: same metal cathode, same intensity.

Procedure

  • Use photocell with metal cathode (e.g. caesium)
  • Illuminate with monochromatic light from set of LEDs / filters with known wavelengths $\lambda$
  • Calculate $f = c/\lambda$
  • Apply variable reverse voltage to photocell, measure with voltmeter
  • Increase voltage until ammeter reads zero — that voltage is $V_0$
  • Repeat for 6+ frequencies

Safety

Avoid looking directly into UV LEDs — wear UV-protective goggles.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: hard to determine exact zero current (small thermionic current). Imp: use sensitive picoammeter and extrapolate I-V curve to zero.

④ Diffraction grating Wave optics

Problem. For a diffraction grating, $d \sin\theta = n\lambda$. Plan an experiment to determine the wavelength $\lambda$ of laser light.

Linearization

$\sin\theta = \dfrac{\lambda}{d} \cdot n$ → plot $\sin\theta$ vs $n$:

$$\lambda = d \cdot \text{gradient}$$

Variables

Independent: $n$ (order of maximum). Dependent: $\theta$. Constants: $d$ (grating spacing), $\lambda$.

Procedure

  • Mount laser, grating, and screen on optical bench
  • Measure distance $D$ from grating to screen
  • Measure positions of 1st, 2nd, 3rd... order maxima from central spot: $y_n$
  • Calculate $\theta_n = \tan^{-1}(y_n / D)$
  • Find $\sin\theta_n$ for each $n$
  • Repeat measurements 3 times to find mean

Safety

Never look directly into the laser beam — point laser away from people.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: central spot too bright to see exact centre. Imp: measure between symmetric orders ($+n$ and $-n$), divide by 2.

⑤ Radioactive decay Nuclear physics

Problem. The activity of a radioactive source follows: $A = A_0 e^{-\lambda t}$. Plan an experiment to determine the decay constant $\lambda$ and the half-life $T_{1/2}$.

Linearization

Take the natural log: $\ln A = -\lambda t + \ln A_0$ → plot $\ln A$ vs $t$:

  • Gradient = $-\lambda$ → $\lambda = -\text{gradient}$
  • $T_{1/2} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \dfrac{0.693}{\lambda}$

Variables

Independent: $t$ (time since start). Dependent: $A$ (count rate). Constants: distance from source to detector, geometry.

Procedure

  • Place GM tube at fixed distance from source
  • Measure background count rate first (10 min, no source) → subtract from all readings
  • Place source, record count for fixed interval (e.g. 60 s) every 5 min
  • Record count at least 6 different times across at least one half-life
  • Subtract background, take $\ln$

Safety

Use long tongs to handle radioactive source; keep source at maximum distance when not in use; wear lab coat and gloves.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: random nature of decay → high uncertainty in count rate. Imp: count for longer intervals (5 min instead of 1 min) at each time, calculate average rate.

⑥ Surface tension Molecular physics

Problem. The force needed to detach a ring of radius $r$ from a water surface is: $F = 4\pi r \gamma$. Plan an experiment to determine the surface tension coefficient $\gamma$.

Linearization

Already linear: plot $F$ vs $r$ → gradient = $4\pi\gamma$, so $\gamma = \text{gradient}/(4\pi)$.

Procedure

  • Use rings of different known radii (measure each with vernier caliper, average over several positions)
  • Hang ring from sensitive Newton meter / digital force sensor
  • Lower ring slowly until just touching water surface (tare/zero the meter at this point)
  • Slowly raise the support; record max force just before ring detaches
  • Repeat 3 times per ring, take mean
  • Use distilled water at constant temperature

Safety

Wipe up water spills immediately.

Limitation / Improvement

Lim: hard to keep ring exactly horizontal → asymmetric pull. Imp: use 3-point suspension and check horizontality with spirit level.

Uncertainties — how to calculate them

Uncertainties appear in every single Q2 answer. If you mess them up, you lose marks on the table, graph, gradient, intercept, and final answer. This tab covers every type you will see on the exam, with worked examples and interactive practice.

Three types of uncertainty basics

Absolute

The raw ± value in the same units as the measurement.

$L = 25.0 \pm 0.5$ cm

The ±0.5 cm is the absolute uncertainty.

Fractional

Absolute uncertainty divided by the value. No units.

$\dfrac{\Delta L}{L} = \dfrac{0.5}{25.0} = 0.020$

Percentage

Fractional uncertainty × 100%.

$\dfrac{\Delta L}{L} \times 100\% = 2.0\%$
Rule of thumb: use fractional/percentage when combining uncertainties (multiplication, division, powers). Use absolute when adding or subtracting quantities.

Rules for combining uncertainties core rules

Rule 1: Addition / Subtraction add absolutes

If $z = a + b$ or $z = a - b$:

$$\Delta z = \Delta a + \Delta b$$

Example

Extension of spring = final length − original length:
$L_1 = 40.0 \pm 0.5$ cm, $L_2 = 25.0 \pm 0.5$ cm
$x = L_1 - L_2 = 15.0$ cm
$\Delta x = 0.5 + 0.5 = \pm 1.0$ cm
Result: $x = 15.0 \pm 1.0$ cm

Rule 2: Multiplication / Division add fractionals

If $z = a \times b$ or $z = a / b$:

$$\frac{\Delta z}{z} = \frac{\Delta a}{a} + \frac{\Delta b}{b}$$

Example

Speed $v = d / t$:
$d = 2.50 \pm 0.05$ m, $t = 1.20 \pm 0.30$ s
$v = 2.50 / 1.20 = 2.083$ m/s
$\frac{\Delta v}{v} = \frac{0.05}{2.50} + \frac{0.30}{1.20} = 0.020 + 0.250 = 0.270$
$\Delta v = 0.270 \times 2.083 = 0.56$ m/s
Result: $v = 2.1 \pm 0.6$ m/s

Rule 3: Powers multiply fractional by power

If $z = a^n$:

$$\frac{\Delta z}{z} = n \cdot \frac{\Delta a}{a}$$

Example

Area of circle $A = \pi r^2$:
$r = 5.0 \pm 0.1$ cm
$A = \pi \times 5.0^2 = 78.5$ cm²
$\frac{\Delta A}{A} = 2 \times \frac{0.1}{5.0} = 0.040 = 4.0\%$
$\Delta A = 0.040 \times 78.5 = 3.1$ cm²
Result: $A = 79 \pm 3$ cm²

Rule 4: Reciprocal (1/x) same fractional

If $z = 1/a$, this is the same as $z = a^{-1}$, so:

$$\frac{\Delta z}{z} = \frac{\Delta a}{a} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \Delta\!\left(\frac{1}{a}\right) = \frac{1}{a} \cdot \frac{\Delta a}{a} = \frac{\Delta a}{a^2}$$

Example (this comes up every year in Q2!)

$I = 130 \pm 2$ mA. Find $1/I$ with uncertainty.
$1/I = 1/130 = 0.00769$ mA¹ = $7.69$ A¹
$\frac{\Delta(1/I)}{1/I} = \frac{2}{130} = 0.0154$
$\Delta(1/I) = 0.0154 \times 7.69 = 0.12$ A¹
Result: $1/I = 7.7 \pm 0.1$ A¹

Alternative method: calculate $1/I_{\min}$ and $1/I_{\max}$, then $\Delta = \frac{1}{2}(1/I_{\min} - 1/I_{\max})$.
$1/128 = 7.81$, $1/132 = 7.58$ → $\Delta = (7.81 - 7.58)/2 = 0.12$ — same answer.
Rule 5: Constants don't add uncertainty important

Pure numbers like $\pi$, $2$, $4$, $g = 9.81$ (given constant) have zero uncertainty. Only measured quantities contribute.

Example

$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$, find $\Delta T$:
Only $L$ is measured. $2$, $\pi$, $g$ are exact/given.
$T = kL^{1/2}$ → $\frac{\Delta T}{T} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{\Delta L}{L}$

Gradient & intercept uncertainty from a graph Q2 essential

This is worth 4 marks in Q2 (2 for gradient uncertainty, 2 for intercept uncertainty). Here is the exact method:

  1. Draw two lines on your graph

    Best fit line: goes through all error bars, points balanced above and below.
    Worst acceptable line: steepest or shallowest line that still passes through all error bars. It must touch the error bar of the first and last point (or close).

  2. Calculate gradient of each line

    Pick two points far apart on the line (at least half the line length). Use the line, not data points.

    $$\text{grad}_{\text{best}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \qquad \text{grad}_{\text{worst}} = \frac{y_4 - y_3}{x_4 - x_3}$$
  3. Uncertainty in gradient
    $$\Delta(\text{gradient}) = |\text{grad}_{\text{best}} - \text{grad}_{\text{worst}}|$$
  4. Find y-intercept of each line

    Pick any point $(x_0, y_0)$ on the best-fit line, then: $c = y_0 - m \cdot x_0$. Repeat for worst line.

  5. Uncertainty in y-intercept
    $$\Delta c = |c_{\text{best}} - c_{\text{worst}}|$$
Common mistake: using data points instead of points on the line to calculate gradient. Always pick points that lie on the drawn line, not the original data.

Common mistakes to avoid warning

  • Subtracting uncertainties when subtracting values. No! Uncertainties always ADD. $\Delta(a-b) = \Delta a + \Delta b$, not $\Delta a - \Delta b$.
  • Using data points instead of line points for gradient. The gradient comes from the drawn line, not from the table values.
  • Forgetting to convert units. If $I$ is in mA and you need $1/I$ in A¹, convert first: $130$ mA $= 0.130$ A.
  • Too many significant figures in uncertainty. Round $\Delta$ to 1 significant figure (sometimes 2). Then round the value to match.
  • Mixing up absolute and percentage. When multiplying/dividing, use fractional. When adding/subtracting, use absolute. Never mix them.
  • Ignoring the worst acceptable line. This is worth 2+ marks. Always draw it and always use it to find $\Delta(\text{gradient})$ and $\Delta c$.
