Magnification Calculations
▼

Formula

Magnification (M) = Image size (I) ÷ Actual size (A)
Actual size (A) = Image size (I) ÷ Magnification (M)

Step-by-step Method

01
Measure the image with a ruler (in mm or µm)
02
Convert units — use the scale bar on the micrograph to find real size. 1 mm = 1000 µm; 1 µm = 1000 nm
03
Calculate: M = image size ÷ actual size (both in same units)
04
No units — magnification is a ratio, not a measurement

Eyepiece Graticule Method (Paper 2)

01
Find how much of the stage micrometer the graticule covers (e.g. 7 divisions = 0.07 mm = 70 µm)
02
Value per graticule division = 70 µm ÷ 10 = 7 µm
03
Count graticule divisions occupied by specimen; multiply by value per division
Always show all working. State the formula, substitute values with units, give answer with correct unit (usually µm).
Osmosis in Cells
▼
OSMOSISThe net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential through a partially permeable membrane.

Water Potential (Ψ)

Ψ (cell) = Ψs (solute potential) + Ψp (pressure potential)
Pure water: Ψ = 0 kPa (highest possible)
Adding solutes lowers water potential (makes it more negative)
Pressure potential (Ψp) is positive in turgid cells; zero in plasmolysed cells

Cells in Different Solutions

SolutionAnimal cellPlant cell
Hypotonic (dilute)Swells → lysis (bursts)Swells → turgid (wall prevents burst)
IsotonicNo changeNo change (flaccid)
Hypertonic (concentrated)Shrinks → crenationPlasmolysis (membrane pulls from wall)
Liver cells have no cell wall — they lyse in pure water. Plant cells become turgid (not lysed) because the cell wall provides inward pressure potential.

Antigen–Antibody Reactions
▼
ANTIGENA molecule (usually a protein or glycoprotein on a pathogen's surface) that triggers an immune response.
ANTIBODY (IMMUNOGLOBULIN)A Y-shaped glycoprotein produced by plasma cells (B lymphocytes) that binds to a specific antigen via the variable region (paratope).

Antibody Structure

4 polypeptide chains: 2 heavy + 2 light, linked by disulfide bonds
Variable region (paratope) — unique antigen-binding site; complementary to specific antigen
Constant region — same in all antibodies of a class; binds to immune cells
Idiotope — unique variable domain; paratope — specific antigen-binding part

Mechanism of Antigen–Antibody Interaction

01
Antigen enters body → recognised as foreign
02
B cells with complementary receptor are activated → clonal selection
03
B cells differentiate into plasma cells (secrete antibodies) and memory cells
04
Antibody binds antigen → antigen-antibody complex (lock-and-key specificity)
05
Complex triggers destruction: agglutination, phagocytosis, or complement activation

Irreversible Inhibition

Sarin forms a covalent bond with serine residue in the active site of acetylcholinesterase
ACh cannot be broken down → accumulates in synapse → continuous nerve firing
Symptoms: muscle spasms, paralysis, excessive secretions

Translation (Protein Synthesis)
▼

Ribosome Sites

A site (aminoacyl) — where charged tRNA brings the next amino acid
P site (peptidyl) — holds the growing polypeptide chain
E site (exit) — empty tRNA leaves here
Peptidyl transferase — catalyses peptide bond formation between amino acids

Stages of Translation

01
Initiation — small ribosome subunit binds to 5' cap; moves to AUG (start codon); large subunit assembles; Met-tRNA enters P site
02
Elongation — tRNA with anticodon complementary to codon enters A site; peptide bond forms; ribosome translocates 3 nucleotides along mRNA (one codon); empty tRNA exits E site; cycle repeats
03
Termination — stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) reached; release factor enters A site; polypeptide released; ribosome disassembles

Properties of the Genetic Code

Triplet — 3 bases = 1 codon = 1 amino acid
Degenerate — most amino acids coded by multiple codons (64 codons, only 20 amino acids)
Non-overlapping — each base is part of only one codon
Universal — same code in virtually all organisms (evidence of common ancestry)
Unambiguous — each codon codes for only one amino acid
To find the amino acid sequence: mRNA codons → use genetic code table → name amino acids. The non-template DNA strand has same bases as mRNA (T instead of U).

Sodium-Potassium Pump & Membrane Potential
▼

Na⁺-K⁺ Pump Mechanism

01
3 Na⁺ ions bind to pump protein from inside the cell
02
ATP is hydrolysed → ADP + Pi; phosphate attaches to pump, causing shape change
03
3 Na⁺ released outside the cell
04
2 K⁺ bind from outside; Pi released → pump returns to original shape
05
2 K⁺ released inside the cell

Membrane Potential

RESTING MEMBRANE POTENTIALThe electrical potential difference across the membrane of a resting neurone: approximately −70 mV (inside negative relative to outside). Maintained by the Na⁺-K⁺ pump creating unequal ion distribution.

Action Potential Sequence

01
Depolarisation — stimulus opens Na⁺ channels; Na⁺ rushes in; membrane becomes less negative (−70 → +40 mV)
02
Repolarisation — Na⁺ channels close; K⁺ channels open; K⁺ rushes out; membrane returns toward −70 mV
03
Hyperpolarisation — briefly more negative than resting potential; K⁺ channels slowly close
04
Refractory period — Na⁺ channels inactivated; action potential cannot be re-triggered (ensures one-way propagation)
The Na-K pump is essential for maintaining resting potential. It pumps out 3 Na⁺ for every 2 K⁺ brought in, keeping the inside negative.

Ecology

Species diversity · Ecosystem resilience · Statistical analysis

Biodiversity & Ecosystem Resilience
▼
SPECIES DIVERSITYMeasure of both the number of species (richness) and the relative abundance of each species in a community.
SIMPSON'S DIVERSITY INDEX (D)D = 1 − Σ(n/N)² where n = number of individuals of each species, N = total individuals. Higher D = greater diversity.

Diversity → Resilience

Higher species diversity → more complex food webs → greater ecosystem resilience
If one species is lost, others can fill its ecological role (redundancy)
Low diversity → ecosystem vulnerable to disease, climate change, or species loss

Statistical Methods

χ² test — compares observed vs expected frequency; tests if differences are significant or due to chance
t-test — compares means of two sets of data; determines if difference is statistically significant
p < 0.05 → result is statistically significant (less than 5% probability it occurred by chance)

Light-Dependent Reactions (Z-Scheme)
▼

Non-Cyclic Photophosphorylation

01
PS II absorbs light → chlorophyll P680 excited → electrons raised to higher energy level
02
Photolysis of water: 2H₂O → 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ + O₂ (electrons replace those lost from P680)
03
Excited electrons pass through electron transport chain (plastoquinone, cytochrome b6f, plastocyanin) → energy used to pump H⁺ across thylakoid membrane
04
H⁺ gradient drives ATP synthesis via ATP synthase (chemiosmosis) → ATP
05
PS I absorbs light → P700 excited → electrons further raised in energy
06
Electrons passed to NADP⁺ reductase → NADPH formed (with H⁺ from stroma)
Overall: 12H₂O + 12NADP⁺ + 18ADP + 18Pi → 6O₂ + 12NADPH + 18ATP

Cyclic Photophosphorylation

Only PS I involved; electrons cycle back to PS I via electron transport chain
Only ATP produced (no NADPH, no O₂)
Used when the cell needs extra ATP but not NADPH
Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)
▼

Three Stages (in Stroma)

01
Carbon fixation — CO₂ combines with RuBP (ribulose bisphosphate, 5C) via RuBisCO enzyme → unstable 6C compound → 2× GP (glycerate-3-phosphate, 3C)
02
Reduction — GP reduced using ATP + NADPH from light reactions → G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, 3C)
03
Regeneration of RuBP — most G3P used to regenerate RuBP using ATP; some G3P used to make glucose, fatty acids, amino acids
3CO₂ + 6NADPH + 9ATP → G3P + 6NADP⁺ + 9ADP + 8Pi

Limiting Factors

Light intensity — limits rate of ATP and NADPH production
CO₂ concentration — limits carbon fixation step (rate of RuBisCO activity)
Temperature — affects enzyme activity (Calvin cycle enzymes); above optimum → denaturation

Why Isolated Mitochondria Need Pyruvate (Not Glucose)

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm (not inside mitochondria)
Isolated mitochondria cannot convert glucose → pyruvate (no glycolytic enzymes)
Adding pyruvate → link reaction occurs inside mitochondria → Krebs + ETC → O₂ consumed
This is exactly what Paper 2 (2025) Q3 tested! Solution A (glucose) = NO decrease in O₂ in isolated mitochondria. Solution B (pyruvate) = O₂ decreases as respiration occurs.

Lac Operon (E. coli)

OPERONA cluster of genes under control of a single promoter, transcribed as a single polycistronic mRNA. Efficient for prokaryotes — switch several related genes on/off simultaneously.
01
No lactose: repressor protein binds operator → RNA polymerase blocked → lac Z, Y, A NOT transcribed
02
Lactose present: allolactose (inducer) binds repressor → repressor changes shape → cannot bind operator
03
RNA polymerase binds promoter → transcribes lac genes → β-galactosidase, permease, transacetylase produced

Eukaryotic Gene Regulation

Promoter — sequence upstream of gene; binding site for RNA polymerase (with transcription factors)
Enhancers — distant DNA sequences that increase transcription when bound by activator proteins
Transcription factors — proteins that bind to promoter/enhancer regions to regulate gene expression
Reporter genes (e.g. GFP, lacZ) — used to study which promoter regions control gene expression; inserted downstream of a promoter to show when/where it is active
Epigenetics
▼
EPIGENETICSHeritable changes in gene expression that do NOT alter the DNA nucleotide sequence. Caused by chemical modifications to DNA or histones.

Mechanisms

DNA methylation — methyl groups (–CH₃) added to cytosine bases (especially CpG islands in promoters) → silences gene expression
Histone modification — acetylation of histones loosens DNA winding → increases transcription. Deacetylation → chromatin condenses → gene silenced
Epigenetic marks can be passed to daughter cells (mitosis) and sometimes to offspring (transgenerational)

Significance

Explains cell differentiation — all cells have same DNA but different genes expressed
Environmental factors (diet, stress, toxins) can alter epigenome
Implicated in cancer (hypermethylation of tumour suppressor genes)

Human Genome Project
▼
HUMAN GENOME PROJECT (HGP)International scientific effort (1990–2003) that sequenced all ~3 billion base pairs of the human genome and identified ~20,000–25,000 genes.

Significance

Medical diagnostics — identify genetic basis of diseases (BRCA1/2 → breast cancer; CFTR → cystic fibrosis)

Gene therapy — replacing defective genes
Evolutionary biology — comparing genomes across species reveals evolutionary relationships
Pharmacogenomics — understanding how genetic variation affects drug response

Biotechnology

Monoclonal antibodies · Recombinant DNA · Cloning · Micropropagation

Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)
▼

Hybridoma Method

01
Immunise a small mammal (mouse) with the target antigen → B cells produce specific antibodies
02
Harvest B cells from spleen
03
Fuse B cells with myeloma cells (immortal cancer cells) → hybridomas
04
HAT selection — grow in HAT medium; only hybridomas survive (myeloma cells die; unfused B cells die)
05
Screen hybridomas for correct antibody production (ELISA)
06
Clone positive hybridoma → grow in bioreactor → harvest mAbs

Why Fuse with Myeloma Cells?

B cells normally have short lifespan and cannot grow indefinitely in culture
Myeloma cells are immortal — can divide indefinitely
Hybridoma = immortality of myeloma + antibody production of B cell

mAbs vs Polyclonal Antibodies

MonoclonalPolyclonal
SpecificitySingle epitopeMultiple epitopes
ConsistencyHighly consistent (same batch = same mAb)Variable between batches
ProductionExpensive; hybridoma requiredCheaper; from immunised animals
UseTargeted cancer therapy, diagnosticsResearch, some diagnostics

Why mAbs May Cause Immune Response in Patients

Mouse-derived mAbs are recognised as foreign antigens by the patient's immune system
Patient produces anti-mouse antibodies → mAb neutralised → treatment ineffective
Solution: humanised mAbs — replace murine constant regions with human sequences

Applications of mAbs

Cancer therapy (e.g. Herceptin/trastuzumab targets HER2 receptor on breast cancer cells)

Steps to Produce Recombinant DNA

01
Isolation — target gene isolated using restriction enzymes (cut at specific recognition sites) OR synthesised using mRNA + reverse transcriptase (→ cDNA)
02
Cut vector — same restriction enzyme cuts plasmid (leaves sticky ends complementary to gene)
03
Ligation — DNA ligase joins gene into plasmid → recombinant plasmid
04
Transformation — plasmid introduced into host cell (E. coli) by heat shock or electroporation
05
Selection — antibiotic resistance markers identify transformed bacteria
06
Expression — bacteria grown in fermenters → express human protein (e.g. insulin, growth hormone)

Plasmid Structure

Small, circular, double-stranded DNA (extrachromosomal)
Contains origin of replication, antibiotic resistance gene(s), multiple cloning site (MCS)
Acts as a vector to carry the gene of interest into host cell

Animal Cloning (Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer — SCNT)

01
Remove nucleus from donor egg cell (enucleate)
02
Insert nucleus from somatic cell of animal to be cloned
03
Stimulate with electric pulse → cell divides (embryo development)
04
Implant embryo into surrogate mother

Plant Micropropagation

01
Take explant (small piece of tissue) from meristem (shoot tip, axillary bud)
02
Surface sterilise explant
03
Culture on sterile agar medium with growth regulators (auxins + cytokinins) → callus formation
04
Alter hormone ratio → adventitious shoots and roots form
05
Transfer to soil → harden off
Human cloning — ethical issues: loss of individual uniqueness; exploitation; unknown long-term effects; religious objections; risk of abnormalities; consent of clone. Legally prohibited in most countries.
